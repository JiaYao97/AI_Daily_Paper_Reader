Generative models are increasingly used to capture correlations in many-body systems, but the representations they learn remain largely opaque to physical interpretation. Here, we establish an intuitive criterion that quantifies the capacity of a variational autoencoder (VAE) to faithfully recon-struct the joint probability distribution of a many body system. In a nutshell, a bound on the VAE capacity is obtained by comparing the rate of the latent channel to the bipartite mutual information of the data. Using this bound, we show that the conditionally independent decoder of any successful VAE is structurally identical to a finite-size mean-field factorization. Hence, a successful reconstruc-tion is direct evidence for a latent mean-field theory and the microscopic parameters of that theory can be read off the trained decoder. We validate these conclusions on a hierarchy of solvable models with scalar (Curie-Weiss), vector (Hopfield) and tensor (Maier-Saupe) order parameters, recovering the full Hopfield pattern matrix from equilibrium samples alone. We find that, when applied to Salamander retinal recordings, a two-latent VAE reproduces the population statistics with only two effective collective variables allowing us to recover the ‘stored patterns’ of the neural population and write a generalized Hopfield model which correctly models the experimental data. 

A long standing challenge in the natural sciences is to predict and understand the behavior of many body systems with complicated correlations among their con-stituents. Iconic examples range from magnetic materials undergoing phase transitions to information processing in networks of neurons. Collective phenomena in com-plex systems are often mathematically intractable and re-quire clever approximations and computational methods. The core mathematical difficulty is to estimate the joint probability distribution p(x) describing a many body sys-tem x = {x1, x 2, · · · , x N }. Typically x is a collection of N variables with complicated correlations resulting from interactions among microscopic constituents. Con-sequently, the probability distribution p(x) is not factor-izable,

For example, by automating the identification of collective variables or order parameters that reduce the dimensionality of the system under investigation [1–4]. Although very powerful, machine learning tools are often applied to scientific problems in a heuristic fashion with-out establishing apriori the conditions that these complex phenomena or datasets need to satisfy for the chosen ML approach to work. Energy-based and generative models such as restricted Boltzmann machines [5–7], variational autoregressive networks [8], diffusion models [9, 10] and Variational Au-toEncoders (VAEs) [11] have been used with great suc-cess in several fields including neuroscience [12], physics [3, 13–19], biophysical modeling [20] and computer sci-ence [11, 21]. VAEs have limitations and known failure cases [22, 23] where they are unable to correctly approx-imate p(x). In this paper, we will show that VAEs are structurally identical to a finite-size mean-field factoriza-tion of the joint distribution, as summarized in Fig. 1. We will derive when VAEs can succeed in describing cor-related systems, or more precisely, when they will nec-essarily fail irrespective of algorithm implementation or hyperparameter selection. The equivalence between VAEs and mean-field mod-els has three consequences. First, low-dimensional VAEs provably fail on systems with no mean-field description, such as the two-dimensional Ising model (Sec. V A). Sec-ond, a successful VAE reconstruction is evidence for a latent mean-field theory, which we demonstrate on the Curie-Weiss (Sec. V A), Hopfield (Sec. V B), and Maier-Saupe (Sec. V C) models, spanning scalar, vector, and tensor order parameters. Third, the microscopic param-eters of the corresponding mean-field theory can be re-covered directly from the trained decoder. We illustrate this by recovering Hopfield patterns from equilibrium spin samples. We conclude by applying these ideas to retinal population recordings under naturalistic stimula-tion [24]. The trained VAE uncovers two low-dimensional mean-field codes organizing the 40 ganglion cells, leading to a generalized Hopfield model [25] with fitted parame-ters which accurately reproduce the experimental data.

> IV. WHEN CAN A VAE FIT p(x)?

The conditional independence assumption of Eq. (6) enforces all message-passing between xi and xj to be routed through z for any i and j. If we cut the en-tire system {1, · · · , N } into two disjoint pieces A ⊔ B =

{1, · · · , N } then all interactions between xA = {xi : i ∈

A} and xB must be mediated by z. Consequently, all the information shared between xA and xB must be con-tained in the joint description of x and z. Mathemati-cally, this yields the inequality 

I(x, z) ≥ I(xA, xB ) , (23) where I(x, y ) = H(x)−H(x|y) is the mutual information of x and y. This inequality holds for any A and B, we can tighten the bound by maximizing 

I(x, z) ≥ Ibip (p) ≡ max  

> A⊔B

I(xA, xB ) . (24) The quantity Ibip (p) is a physical property of the system we study which measures the amount of information re-quired to describe the correlations of the physical system. 

On the other hand, a famous identity, called ELBO surgery, from the VAE literature [34, 35] states 

R = I(x, z) + DKL (qϕ(z)|| pψ (z)) , (25) where 

R ≡ ⟨ DKL (qϕ(z|x)|| pψ (z)⟩p(x) . (26) This equation can be understood as follows. The prior 

pψ (z) can be interpreted as the quantity defining the ‘ge-ometry’ of the latent space. On the other hand, qϕ(z) is the encoder’s approximation of this geometry. The rate 

R measures how ‘uniquely’ each data point x is encoded in the true latent space pψ (z). Uniqueness can come in two forms. Either z contains enough information about 

x that two different samples x, x′ can be discriminated into z, z′, this is measured by I(x, z). Or the approxi-mate latent space qϕ(z) does not match the true latent space pψ (z) making encoded samples trivially ‘unique’, since they will not fit in the true geometry of the latent space. If the VAE encoder is trained properly, this second term should vanish and the approximate geometry qϕ(z)should match the true geometry pψ (z) [34–36]. Then R

can be interpreted as the amount of information that the trained VAE encoder is able to pack into the latent space. 

Matching Eq. (24) and Eq. (25) leads to a criterion necessary for a VAE to be able to successfully model a system 

R ≳ Ibip (p) . (27) In other words, the amount of information R that the VAE is able to encode into the latent space has to be greater than the amount of information Ibip (p) required to describe the correlations of p(x). For a large class of VAE encoders the rate R can be approximated by 

R ≃ d log(1 /σ ) , (28) where d is the dimension of the latent space and σ is the average precision of the VAE encoder. A more formal and precise description of this argument is provided in the Methods. Combining the mean-field description given in Sec. II with Eq. (27) we can now write formally the three main conclusions put forward in this paper. 

C1. If the joint distribution p(x) admits no mean-field description (i.e. its correlations cannot be captured by a couple of low-dimensional order parameters), then Ibip (p) ∼ O (N α) and VAEs with a factoriz-able decoder, low-dimensional latents ( d ∼ O (1)) and a smooth regular prior ( σ̸ = 0) will fail to re-construct p(x). 

C2. If a VAE with a factorizable decoder, low-dimensional latents and smooth regular prior suc-cessfully reconstructs p(x), then p(x) must admit a non-trivial mean-field description. 

C3. If the VAE has been trained successfully, the mi-croscopic mean-field parameters can be recovered from the trained network. The remainder of the paper instantiates each of these three corollaries on a hierarchy of synthetic solvable models (Sec. V) and on experimental retinal recordings (Sec. VI).

> A. Scalar order parameter: Ising versus Curie-Weiss

We first illustrate the equivalence on the two-dimensional Ising and Curie-Weiss models, which have also been studied with VAEs and related generative ar-chitectures in earlier work [3, 13, 14]. However, their analysis is usually restricted to one-point observables (magnetization / susceptibility / binder cumulant). This hides the true failure of the VAEs’ reconstruction of 2D Ising which is contained in two-point functions. We ac-curately quantify this failure mode using TC |z. In two dimensions the Ising model has no mean-field description. Mean-field becomes accurate only in the infinite dimen-sional limit. On the other hand, the Curie-Weiss model is always mean-field by construction but the fully inde-pendent factorization in Eq. (11) becomes valid only in the thermodynamic limit. We generated 10 5 independent two-dimensional Ising equilibrium samples (resp. Curie-Weiss equilibrium samples) for 50 different temperatures ranging from 0 .1 to 3 .5 (with J = 1) using the Wolff cluster algorithm [37] (resp. using direct sampling). We then trained a VAE with a latent dimension of 1 to recon-struct the equilibrium samples. We see in Fig. 1(b,c,d) that the VAE reproduces ⟨| m|⟩ (T ) for CW across the en-tire transition region. The agreement extends to higher moments: at T ≪ Tc the Binder cumulant matches the universal value U ⋆

> VI. EXPERIMENTAL APPLICATION: LATENT STRUCTURE OF RETINAL CODES

Retinal population responses can be captured quan-titatively by maximum-entropy models that are mathe-matically equivalent to Ising models with pairwise inter-actions [24], and analogous constructions extend to larger populations when global activity constraints are included [45]. Starting from the salamander retina recordings of Ref. [24], we assemble 145 repeated presentations of a 26 .5 s natural-movie clip for the same N = 40 ganglion cells (see Methods IX F for preprocessing). Correlations in this preparation are concentrated near a 20 ms time scale [24, 45], so we partition time into consecutive non-overlapping 20 ms bins; in each bin the population state is {x1, x 2, · · · , x 40 } with xi = 1 if neuron i fired and 

xi = −1 otherwise by analogy with our previous spin-systems. Treating successive bins as independent sam-ples, we train a VAE with a linear decoder and with two latent dimensions to reproduce these 40-bit words. The data cannot be described by a fully factorized independent model such as the one given in Eq. (11), since population synchrony and overlaps are poorly de-scribed by this modeling [24] as can be seen in Fig. 5(d-e). Refs. [24, 45] instead fit Ising-like models. These Ising-like models rely on fitting O(N 2) couplings describing the microscopic interactions between all pairs of neurons. In-stead, we show that a two-dimensional latent with a con-ditionally independent decoder can match the empirical count distribution. Only 2 variables suffice to correctly capture the correlations between all N = 40 neurons in-stead of the ∼ 800 couplings learned in Ref. [24]. VII. DISCUSSION 

We have established an equivalence between VAEs and finite-size mean-field approximations from statisti-10 cal mechanics, sharpened by the capacity criterion of Eq. (27). This identification has three consequences: (C1) low-dimensional regular VAEs cannot faithfully rep-resent data with no mean-field description. For exam-ple, the two-dimensional Ising model. (C2) Success on a complex empirical distribution constitutes evidence for a finite-size mean-field description, as we have demon-strated on Curie-Weiss (Sec. V A), Hopfield (Sec. V B), Maier-Saupe (Sec. V C), and retinal population code-words (Sec. VI). (C3) When such a description exists, its underlying microscopic parameters can be read off di-rectly from the trained decoder, which we exemplified by recovering the full Hopfield pattern matrix from the decoder’s logit-space Jacobian and fitting the parame-ters of a generalized Hopfield model to neural population recordings.

> H. Capacity criterion

For any bipartition {1, . . . , N } = A ⊔ B of the sites, the conditional independence ansatz of Eq. (5) implies that the sub-configurations XA and XB are conditionally independent given Z, so XA → Z → XB forms a Markov chain. The data-processing inequality then gives 

I(XA; XB ) ≤ I(XA; Z) ≤ I(X; Z) , (52) for any bipartition A ⊔ B. Maximizing the left-hand side over bipartitions yields our first bound,

(53) Equation (53) is the message-passing bound : all correla-tions between A and B must be routed through the single hub Z, so the rate I(X; Z) of the latent channel is at least the maximum information that must cross any cut. The quantity Ibip (p) is a property of the target distribution alone, so Eq. (53) translates a physical fact about p(x), namely the scaling of its correlations, into a requirement on the network. The rate that the network actually delivers is the KL term of the ELBO in Eq. (3), 

R ≡ ⟨ DKL (qϕ(z | x) ∥ pψ (z)) ⟩p(x) , (54) which coincides with I(X; Z) up to a non-negative dis-crepancy that vanishes at the ELBO optimum [34–36]. For a Gaussian prior pψ (z) = N (0 , Id) and Gaus-sian encoder qϕ(z | x) = N (μ(x), σ (x)2Id) in the near-deterministic regime σ ≪ 1, the dominant term reduces to the sphere-packing capacity

> 1. Going beyond the Gaussian regime

The above mentioned capacity criterion is restricted to a Gaussian prior pψ (z) = N (0 , Id) with a Gaussian poste-rior qϕ(z|x) = N (μ(x), σ (x)2Id) in the near-deterministic regime σ ≪ 1.
