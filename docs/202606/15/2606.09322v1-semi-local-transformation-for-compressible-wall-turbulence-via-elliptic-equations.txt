# Semi-local transformation for compressible wall turbulence via elliptic equations 

Zifei Yin 1†, Jing Wang 1

> 1

School of Aeronautics and Astronautics, Shanghai Jiao Tong University (Received xx; revised xx; accepted xx) 

Compressibility and wall heat transfer change the inner scaling of wall turbulence through the mean density and viscosity fields. Existing semi-local transformations usually act on a wall-normal profile after the profile has been chosen. Here an elliptic semi-local transformation defines the transformed coordinate and transformed velocity before wall-normal profiles are extracted. Ordinary semi-local scaling based on local density and viscosity gives the local part of the wall-normal stretching. A Helmholtz-type elliptic equation then adds a finite-amplitude density-induced correction over a finite wall-layer distance; the corrected stretching gives the transformed coordinate 𝑌 +, and the mean viscous shear gives the transformed velocity vector 

U +. The equations use mean velocity, density, viscosity, wall distance and wall normals. When density and viscosity are uniform, the density term vanishes, 𝑌 + reduces to the ordinary wall coordinate and each transformed velocity component reduces to the conventional mean velocity component in wall units. A single set of wall-layer constants is calibrated from two cooled Zhang-Duan-Choudhari flat plates and then applied to attached zero-pressure-gradient boundary layers and mixed thermal-wall channels. For channel flows, the same elliptic coordinate equation is used on each channel half, from the wall to the centreline, with density measured from the centreline; a cooled shock/boundary-layer interaction is included to examine departure and recovery of the transformed inner layer in a non-equilibrium wall layer. The density-induced coordinate correction reduces the inner- and buffer-layer profile spread in the cooled high-speed boundary layers; in mixed thermal-wall channels the isothermal side receives a larger correction than the adiabatic side. 

Key words: compressible turbulence, turbulent boundary layers, wall-bounded turbulence 

1. Introduction 

In high-speed wall-bounded turbulence, wall cooling and wall heating produce strong wall-normal variation of the mean density and viscosity across the inner layer (Bradshaw 1977; Zhang et al. 2018). The wall units and the mean velocity gradient then depend on Mach number and thermal condition, so a compressible mean profile cannot be compared by incompressible wall-unit scaling alone. This matters for skin friction, wall heat transfer and mean-velocity comparisons. The difficulty is to decide how much of the profile change is

absorbed by ordinary semi-local scaling based on local density and viscosity, and what part of the inner layer still requires a transformation. Semi-local transformations address this question by asking whether the compressible mean profile can be measured in a coordinate that accounts for local density and viscosity. If the transformation is physically appropriate, the viscous sublayer, buffer layer and logarithmic region should approach the same inner-law behaviour over a useful range of Mach number and wall temperature. The transformation is therefore a test of the inner momentum scaling as well as a practical normalisation. The buffer layer is the most sensitive part of this test, because viscous scaling, property variation and the onset of turbulent transport all act over the same wall-normal range. Early transformations acted mainly on velocity. The Van Driest transformation introduced a density-weighted velocity for compressible turbulent boundary layers (Van Driest 1951). Later semi-local scalings used local density and viscosity to define a wall coordinate that changes with the material properties of the mean flow (Huang et al. 1995; Patel et al. 2015, 2016). The Trettel-Larsson transformation then gave a more systematic coordinate-and-velocity transformation for wall turbulence with heat transfer (Trettel & Larsson 2016). This development shows the main trend: the transformation has moved from a velocity correction toward a correction of the wall-normal stretching implied by local material properties. The next step was to bring the mean-stress balance into the transformation. Griffin-Fu-Moin used this balance to obtain strong collapse for many compressible boundary layers and channels (Griffin et al. 2021); the inverse use of such transformations has also entered near-wall modelling for compressible boundary layers (Griffin et al. 2023). Independent assessments and data-driven studies have made the remaining structure clearer: the largest discrepancies often appear in the buffer layer, and the wall thermal condition controls the sign and magnitude of the mismatch after ordinary semi-local scaling based on local density and viscosity (Volpiani et al. 2020; Danis & Durbin 2024). The remaining modelling question is what information, beyond local density and viscosity scaling, is needed to set the transformed inner coordinate. Profile transformations define how a selected wall-normal profile is mapped. In a devel-oping, asymmetric or multi-wall mean flow, the transformed coordinate and velocity must also be specified before the profile is sampled. This distinction is more than geometrical. The density and viscosity fields set the local viscous length point by point, but the buffer-layer displacement left by semi-local scaling is tied to motions with finite wall-normal extent. Neighbouring wall-normal lines therefore need the same wall condition, density-induced coordinate stretching and decay away from the wall. These questions become unavoidable in non-parallel mean flows, and they are already present in the physics of the inner layer. This leads to an elliptic formulation. The transformation is written as a problem for the transformed coordinate with finite wall-normal extent. The wall fixes the origin, the wall-distance normal sets the direction in which the coordinate grows, and an inner length scale sets how far a density-induced change in stretching affects neighbouring wall-normal lines. The mathematical setting is analogous in spirit to differential wall-distance methods, where a geometrical distance is written as a field equation with explicit boundary conditions (Tucker 2003), and to elliptic near-wall modelling, where wall information is spread over a turbulence length scale (Durbin 1991; Manceau & Hanjalic 2002). The unknowns here are the semi-local coordinate, its density-induced coordinate correction and the transformed velocity. The elliptic equation states the semi-local transformation in the wall layer, with a finite wall-layer correction and explicit wall and outer-boundary conditions. The elliptic semi-local transformation proposed here uses mean-flow fields: the mean velocity vector, density, viscosity, wall distance and wall normals. The starting point is the ordinary semi-local scaling based on local density and viscosity. The added step places the Elliptic semi-local transformation 3density effect left by this scaling in the wall-normal coordinate stretching. A Helmholtz-type elliptic equation gives a finite wall-layer correction to that density-induced stretching; the corrected stretching defines 𝑌 +, and the mean viscous shear defines U +. On a wall-normal station, the streamwise or wall-tangential component reduces to the profile quantity used for comparison with Van Driest, Trettel-Larsson and Griffin-Fu-Moin transformations. The DNS comparisons below are one-dimensional sections through the solved transformation. The paper is organised as follows. Section 2 gives the field equations, their physical interpretation, the finite-amplitude density correction and the one-dimensional recovery. Section 3 sets the wall-layer length scales and summarises the DNS flow conditions. The results first show boundary-layer profile collapse and the corresponding transformed coordinate and velocity, followed by comparisons with standard transformations. The centreline-density reference is introduced after the single-wall boundary-layer behaviour has been established. The discussion returns to the range of use and the observed limits of the transformation. 

2. Elliptic semi-local transformation 

2.1. Inner-layer coordinate stretching in semi-local scaling 

In a compressible wall layer, the mean density and viscosity first change the local viscous length. This local part is already contained in ordinary semi-local scaling. The remaining buffer-layer displacement is different. It is tied to finite wall-normal motions and to the no-slip wall that fixes the origin of the inner coordinate. A semi-local transformation written for a mean field should therefore determine the transformed coordinate over the selected near-wall region, not as a separate correction on each sampled profile. The modelling assumption is that the part of the compressibility effect not removed by ordinary semi-local scaling enters mainly through the wall-normal coordinate stretching. The ordinary semi-local stretch is retained, but the density-induced displacement left by that stretch acts over a finite wall-layer distance. The correction must leave the viscous origin unchanged and must fade before it becomes a uniform translation of the logarithmic region. 6. Discussion and range of use 

The comparisons indicate where the density-induced coordinate correction is most useful. In cooled high-speed boundary layers, ordinary semi-local scaling removes the leading change of viscous length but leaves a remaining buffer-layer displacement. The present correction acts on this displacement by changing the wall-normal stretching over a finite inner-layer distance. Among the plotted boundary-layer cases, the largest reduction of buffer-layer spread occurs in the cooled Zhang-Duan-Choudhari cases. When ordinary semi-local scaling or the mean-stress-balance transformation already places the viscous and buffer layers, as in the developing Mach 2.28 boundary layers from the Volpiani-Bernardini-Larsson data, the elliptic correction remains small. Solving the transformation over the two-dimensional wall layer is useful in non-parallel or multi-wall flows. The transformed coordinate and velocity are defined before a wall-normal station is chosen, so neighbouring profiles share the same wall origin, wall-normal direction and coordinate field. In the shock/boundary-layer interaction, the transformed profiles leave the attached-flow inner-layer band under the upstream pressure-gradient influence and through the interaction, then return toward it as the wall shear recovers downstream. This behaviour is consistent with a transformation based on mean density and viscosity rather than on the full non-equilibrium momentum balance. The range of use is therefore controlled by the wall-normal structure of density and viscosity and by the state of the wall layer, not by Mach number alone. Strong near-wall property variation with an attached or recovering inner layer is the favourable case. Weak property variation, late hot-wall development, separated interaction regions and mixed thermal-wall channels are more restrictive. In the mixed channels, the centreline-density reference corrects the isothermal side more strongly than the adiabatic side, showing that the reference state matters once two unequal wall layers share an outer state. The present comparisons also assume a uniform wall reference state on each wall considered. Flows with streamwise-varying wall temperature, blowing, ablation or reacting walls would require the wall values 

𝜌 𝑤 and 𝜇 𝑤 to vary along the surface and to enter the local wall scaling consistently. 

7. Conclusions 

An elliptic semi-local coordinate-and-velocity transformation has been formulated for compressible wall turbulence. It defines 𝑌 + and U + over the near-wall region before a wall-normal profile has been selected. The formulation starts from the ordinary semi-local scaling, adds a finite-amplitude correction to the density-induced coordinate stretching left by local scaling and then obtains the transformed velocity vector from the same corrected stretching and mean viscous shear. For constant properties, the equations reduce to the identity transformation for each velocity component with zero wall velocity; in one-dimensional profile form the streamwise or wall-tangential component recovers the integral formulas used in the DNS comparisons. In the attached cases examined here, the clearest gain occurs in cooled high-speed boundary layers, where the density contrast is strong and concentrated in the inner layer. The developing Mach 2.28 boundary layers change less, consistent with ordinary semi-local or stress-balance transformations already carrying much of the profile variation. The mixed thermal-wall channels show the main limitation of the present reference choice: using the centreline density corrects the isothermal side more strongly, whereas the adiabatic side retains the broadest spread among the channel profiles shown. The cooled-wall shock/boundary-layer interaction tests the transformation in a non-equilibrium wall layer. The transformation is solved over the two-dimensional wall layer 18 Yin and Wang 

before station profiles are extracted. The profiles then show departure under upstream pressure-gradient influence and through the interaction, followed by a renewed approach to the transformed inner-layer band at the recovered downstream stations. This supports using the PDE solution to follow inner-layer departure and recovery in non-equilibrium flows. Stress-balance and ordinary semi-local profile transformations remain the natural tools when a wall-normal profile is already defined and contains the information needed for collapse. The elliptic semi-local transformation is intended for cases in which the transformed coordinate and velocity must be obtained from the mean velocity, density and viscosity fields before profiles are chosen. Further tests should target mean flows with crossflow, curvature, pressure gradient, separation or interacting walls. Because 𝑌 + and U + are defined as fields, the same equations can also be tested in turbulence modelling. An incompressible near-wall RANS closure could, in principle, be evaluated in the transformed wall coordinate and velocity variables, while density, viscosity and the energy equation remain part of the compressible mean-flow solver. This would not replace compressibility modelling for shocks, heat transfer or outer-layer non-equilibrium, but it would allow the range of incompressible wall-turbulence closures to be assessed in variable-property wall layers.
