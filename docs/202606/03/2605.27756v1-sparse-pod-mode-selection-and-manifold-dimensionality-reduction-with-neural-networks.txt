Abstract. High-performance computing enables simulation of high-dimensional physical sys-tems, but downstream analyses such as inverse problems and control remain computationally ex-pensive, motivating model order reduction (MOR) to construct efficient low-dimensional surrogates. Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), a widely adopted data-driven MOR method, projects dynamics onto linear subspaces spanned by the most energetic modes. However, POD struggles for problems with slowly decaying Kolmogorov n-widths, such as advection-dominated and turbu-lent flows, requiring many modes for accurate reconstruction. Moreover, energy-based selection can discard crucial low-energy modes needed to capture small-scale features. Recent nonlinear mani-fold methods using polynomial mappings with alternating or greedy mode selection achieve better reconstruction with fewer modes. However, these methods fix the nonlinear mapping form a pri-ori, limiting expressivity. Conversely, neural network (NN) manifolds offer greater expressivity but employ energy-based selection. We present SparseModesNet, a dimensionality reduction framework that employs linear encoding via POD modes and nonlinear NN decoding. The decoder leverages LassoNet, a method enforcing hierarchical sparsity through residual connections with linear skip layers, to simultaneously select informative POD modes and learn a nonlinear mapping that mini-mizes reconstruction error. On benchmark advection-dominated and chaotic flows, SparseModesNet matches or exceeds state-of-the-art performance. For turbulent channel flow at friction Reynolds number Re ŌĄ = 5200, we reduce reconstruction error by 51‚Äď78% compared to existing polynomial manifold methods while maintaining interpretability through physically meaningful mode selection.

1. Introduction. Many computational tasks in science and engineering require solving high-dimensional partial differential equations (PDEs), which remains com-putationally expensive despite advances in high-performance computing. Model or-der reduction (MOR) methods address this by creating low-dimensional surrogate models that accurately reconstruct high-dimensional dynamics at significantly re-duced cost, enabling downstream analyses such as inverse problems, control, and optimization [7, 51, 40, 2]. Among these, Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) has emerged as a widely used approach that identifies high-energy modes in data and constructs efficient reduced-order models (ROMs) by projecting dynamics onto a low-dimensional subspace spanned by these modes [33, 56, 8]. However, POD‚Äôs limitation to linear subspaces causes it to underperform for transport-dominated, advection-dominated, or turbulent systems. In such cases, slow spectral decay, known as the Kolmogorov n-width barrier, requires retaining prohibi-tively many modes for accuracy, undermining the primary goal of MOR [18]. Recent work has proposed addressing this barrier by moving beyond linear subspaces to non-linear manifolds that allow nonlinear combinations of POD modes [1, 48].

Some researchers have explored nonlinear manifold representations that augment linear POD subspaces with quadratic [50, 69, 35, 57] or higher-order polynomial mappings [26]. These approaches have successfully constructed ROMs for advection-dominated problems via manifold projection [5] and nonintrusive inference (i.e., with-out access to underlying code) [28], demonstrating potential to overcome the Kol-mogorov barrier with fewer modes. However, many rely on heuristic energy-based mode selection, where modes are ordered by energy retention and then truncated at fixed thresholds. This selection can be suboptimal since low-energy modes may be critical to capture dynamics such as energy dissipation in turbulent flows [4, 12]. Recent works have proposed other mode selection strategies. In [26, 27], the authors define a polynomial mapping that decodes a reduced POD representation and optimize both the linear basis and polynomial coefficients through an alternating minimization scheme, while [60] proposes a greedy algorithm that iteratively selects modes based on their contribution to reducing state reconstruction error, later ex-tended to sparse data in [58]. While advancing beyond energy-based truncation, these methods face two limitations. First, to capture increasingly complex dynamics with higher accuracy requires higher-degree polynomials; including all cross-terms among the reduced variables then incurs combinatorial growth in the number of coefficients, creating a tradeoff between expressivity and computational tractability. Second, mode selection is performed separately from the mapping optimization rather than jointly, which may yield suboptimal mode-mapping combinations. Importantly, while the polynomial coefficients are optimized, the functional form of the mapping (e.g., qua-dratic) is prescribed a priori rather than learned from data, which limits expressivity. Neural networks (NNs) offer an alternative approach to overcoming the Kol-mogorov n-width barrier by learning nonlinear manifold representations directly from data. A prevalent architecture is the autoencoder (AE), which consists of an encoder that compresses high-dimensional states into a low-dimensional latent representation and a decoder that reconstructs the original state from this representation. Unlike polynomial mappings with predetermined functional forms, AEs learn both the com-pression and reconstruction mappings from data, enabling greater flexibility in cap-turing complex nonlinear structure. The work [42] first applied deep convolutional AEs to construct trial manifolds for MOR, demonstrating improved reconstruction over linear methods for advection-dominated problems. Subsequent developments have extended this framework in several directions: [55] incorporates hyper-reduction techniques to lower online computational costs, [66] generalizes convolutional archi-tectures to unstructured meshes common in finite element discretizations, and [14] introduces adaptive decoder filters that evolve with the latent state. Despite these advances, AE-based methods encode information into latent variables that lack direct physical meaning, defying interpretability of the learned representations. In the context of MOR, interpretability can be considered the ability to under-stand which physical features or modes the reduced model captures and how they con-tribute to the reconstruction [15]. Several works have pursued this goal by imposing structure on the learned latent space according to physical considerations: [36] employs self-supervised learning to produce embeddings distinguishable according to physical parameters, [63] uses ő≤-variational AEs to learn disentangled, near-orthogonal latent variables, and [70] encodes frequency information to yield latents corresponding to distinct temporal scales. While these methods endow the latent space with meaning-ful structure, the resulting representations remain dense‚ÄĒall latent coordinates con-tribute to every reconstruction, making it difficult to isolate which modes are essential for a given state. A complementary principle from interpretable machine learning is SPARSE MODE SELECTING NEURAL NET 3that sparsity , the use of fewer active features, enhances interpretability by enabling direct examination of individual contributions [65]. In the MOR setting, sparse rep-resentations that activate only a subset of modes would allow practitioners to identify precisely which physical structures are necessary for accurate reconstruction. This work bridges interpretable nonlinear manifolds with sparse mode selection and expressive NN decoders. We adapt LassoNet [43], a framework enforcing hierar-chical feature sparsity where features participate in NNs only if corresponding linear skip weights are active, to transform the combinatorial mode selection problem into a continuous regularization path promoting sparsity during NN training. While Las-soNet remains underexplored in scientific computing beyond limited applications such as physics-informed neural networks for inverse problems [44], we demonstrate its effi-cacy for mode selection with a NN decoder, enabling automated, scalable selection of minimal sets of physically interpretable POD modes while leveraging NN expressivity. The contributions of this work are: 

‚ÄĘ We propose SparseModesNet, a novel framework that combines the inter-pretability of POD modes with NN expressivity, enabling sparse mode selec-tion during NN training and accurate reconstruction of dynamical systems with slowly decaying Kolmogorov n-widths. 

‚ÄĘ We demonstrate SparseModesNet‚Äôs effectiveness on benchmark problems and turbulent channel flow, showcasing its ability to select minimal mode sets while maintaining high state and mode reconstruction accuracy. 

‚ÄĘ We analyze the interpretability of our NN decoder by computing modes of the reconstructed data and provide physical insights into selected modes based on their order and characteristics. Several recent works have also combined NN expressivity with physically meaning-ful POD bases for interpretable nonlinear decoders. For example, [20, 6] employ small linear POD bases augmented with NN correction terms, preserving interpretability while harnessing nonlinear expressivity. The authors of [64] extend this approach by applying NN corrections to both encoder and decoder, each weighted with trainable coefficients for improved fitting. Alternatively, some works augment linear reduction with nonlinear manifolds derived from probabilistic embeddings [29], user-defined re-producing kernel Hilbert spaces [21], or Gaussian processes [3]. Although these hybrid approaches maintain physical interpretability and sparsity by selecting few modes, they rely on energy-based heuristics rather than simultaneously identifying essential modes from a candidate set and learning the nonlinear manifold. Another related work [19] recently introduced Decomposed Sparse Modal Opti-mization (DESMO), which leverages the Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) framework [11, 13, 25] to discover interpretable governing equations by pro-moting sparsity in candidate function libraries. DESMO applies SINDy to POD or AE-learned bases by constructing a nonlinear candidate library from modes using predefined basis functions and employing ‚Ąď1-regularization to select the fewest ac-tive terms. While DESMO promotes sparsity in functional representations of a given modal basis, our approach identifies which modes themselves should be retained and simultaneously learns an expressive NN manifold tailored to those selected modes. 3.2.1. Design Choices and Their Theoretical Justification. SparseMod-esNet differs from the original LassoNet and simple ‚Ąď1-regularization in two key re-spects, each essential for guaranteeing monotonic mode elimination (i.e., eliminated modes are never reactivated) when training via the continuation strategy. 

Remark 3.1 (Input masking). Unlike the original LassoNet (2.6), which inputs the full feature vector ňÜ z to the NN, we employ ŌČ ‚äô ňÜz as the NN input such that the unselected modes are effectively zero. Here, ŌČ remains a trainable parameter optimized via (3.2), while ŌČ ‚äô ňÜz serves as the parametrized input to hNN (¬∑).

Hence, we postpone the formal statement and proof of monotonic mode elimination, including the precise regularity assumptions to Section SM1.

Neural Network Architecture. For hNN , we employ a hybrid architecture consist-ing of a single fully connected layer followed by a Deep Polynomial NN (ő†-Net) [16]. The fully connected layer transforms input modes into a representation suitable for the ő†-Net to capture polynomial nonlinearities and serves as the gate layer where the hierarchical sparsity constraint is applied during training. The ő†-Net then models high-order polynomial functions, enabling rich representation of complex relation-ships. While this layer can be extended to multiple fully connected layers for more intricate problems, a single layer proved sufficient for the datasets considered. We experimented with other architectures, including standard multi-layer per-ceptrons, convolutional neural networks, and U-Nets, but found the hybrid fully connected-ő†-Net architecture provided superior reconstruction accuracy and mode selection for our problems. Further details on the hybrid ő†-Net architecture are pro-vided in Section SM3.

Achieving approximately 6 .6 √ó 10 ‚ąí4 or 8 .7 √ó 10 ‚ąí3 relative error with only 15 modes‚ÄĒover 51% and 78% error reduction versus existing non-linear approaches‚ÄĒrepresents significant advance in computational efficiency. Crit-ically, SparseModesNet achieves this while using lower-dimensional nonlinear map-ping ( p = 400) compared to generic cubic mapping ( p = 680) across all experiments (see Table 4), reducing inference costs. The comparable performance of selected ver-sus leading modes suggests that for large-scale turbulent systems, investing computa-tional effort in training high-quality nonlinear decoders may be more beneficial than exhaustive mode selection optimization, though learned mode selection can provide improved accuracy and valuable physical insights into multi-scale turbulent structure.

SM3. Hybrid Neural Network Architecture. In this section, we provide additional details on the NN architecture employed in our proposed SparseMod-esNet framework. SM3.1. Deep Polynomial Neural Networks. Deep Polynomial NNs, or ő†-Nets, introduced in [SM2], are a class of function approximators where the output is a high-order polynomial of the input. This approach contrasts with standard Deep Convolutional NNs (DCNNs), which typically rely on a composition of linear operators and nonlinear activation functions. While any smooth function can be approximated by a polynomial, a direct imple-mentation faces a combinatorial explosion in the number of parameters, which grows polynomially with the order of the approximation, i.e., O(oN ) for an o-dimensional input and an N th -order polynomial. To overcome this challenge, ő†-Nets cast the problem of parameter estimation as a coupled tensor factorization. That is, by decomposing the high-order tensors that represent the polynomial‚Äôs parameters and enforcing factor sharing, the model significantly reduces the number of learnable parameters and enables an efficient im-plementation through hierarchical neural network architectures. > Fig. SM4 .Schematic of the hybrid architecture combining a fully connected layer with a ő†-Net. The input first passes through a fully connected layer, which is then fed into the ő†-Net to produce the final output. This architecture leverages the strengths of both linear transformations and polynomial approximations for enhanced expressivity.

This design choice is necessary because applying the hierarchical sparsity con-straint directly to the CP factors of the ő†-Net would be non-trivial and could com-plicate the training process. By placing the sparsity constraint on the fully connected layer, we can effectively control the selection of modes while still benefiting from the polynomial expressivity of the ő†-Net. N.B., we found this shallow hybrid architecture consisting of a single fully con-nected layer with no nonlinear activation functions followed by a ő†-Net to perform best in our experiments compared to deeper architectures with multiple fully con-nected layers before the ő†-Net or even adding fully connected layers after the ő†-Net. This suggests that complicated architectures are not necessary when the goal is to reconstruct data from a limited number of modes, as the polynomial nature of the ő†-Net already provides sufficient expressivity.
