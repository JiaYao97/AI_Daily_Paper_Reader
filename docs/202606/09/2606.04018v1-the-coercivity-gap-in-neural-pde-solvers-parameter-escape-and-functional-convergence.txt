Abstract 

We study neural approximation of elliptic PDE solutions from a variational perspective. The central point is the distinction between the geometry of neural parameters and the convergence of the corresponding physical states. Even when the original elliptic energy is coercive and strictly convex in the natural energy space, its restriction to a nonlinear neural ansatz may fail to be coercive in parameter space. This failure is caused by non-closedness of neural approximation manifolds and by condensation of neurons, which may generate limiting profiles outside the fixed ansatz class. Nevertheless, the associated state functions may remain bounded and converge strongly to the exact PDE solution. We prove this mechanism for Gaussian wave-packet approximations of a prototypical elliptic model in the whole space, derive convergence rates, and explain how the same state-level stability principle applies to residual minimization methods of PINN type, and HYCO-type hybrid methods. We also discuss relaxation and Tikhonov regularization. 

Keywords. Neural PDE solvers; variational methods; coercivity; Gaussian approximation; finite element method; residual minimization; PINNs; relaxation; Tikhonov regularization. 

# 1 Introduction 

Neural-network-based methods for the numerical approximation of partial differential equations (PDEs) have attracted considerable attention in recent years. Physics-Informed Neural Net-works (PINNs) approximate PDE solutions through residual minimization involving governing equations, boundary conditions, and available data. Variational approaches such as the Deep Ritz method exploit the underlying energy functional whenever available, while more recent Deep Riesz formulations seek to align residual minimization with the natural functional frame-work of the PDE. These developments have generated a rapidly growing literature on approx-imation, convergence, generalization, optimization, and error analysis for neural PDE solvers [16, 7, 18, 5, 15, 3]. Neural solvers offer considerable flexibility and are particularly attractive in high-dimensional or mesh-free settings. At the same time, replacing classical linear approximation spaces by non-linear neural ansätze fundamentally changes the geometry of the approximation problem. The 1

The central theme of this article is the distinction between the behavior of neural parame-ters and that of the corresponding PDE states. Minimizing sequences in parameter space may diverge, become singular, or fail to converge because of the nonlinear geometry of the neural representation, while the associated physical states may nevertheless converge strongly to the true PDE solution in the natural energy space. We refer to this mismatch as a coercivity gap 

between parameter space and physical-state space. To isolate and analyze this phenomenon, we consider a simple variational elliptic model with shallow Gaussian neural ansätze. The goal is not to advocate this specific architecture, but rather to expose, in a transparent setting, structural mechanisms that also arise in more general PDE models and neural architectures: loss of convexity, lack of coercivity in parameter space, neuron condensation, non-closedness of approximation manifolds, relaxation and convexification, and the distinction between parameter convergence and physical-state convergence. 

Main results. The main contributions of the paper are the following. First, we show that fi-nite Gaussian neural manifolds may fail to be closed in the natural energy topology and that this non-closedness produces parameter escape and possible non-attainment of the reduced varia-tional problem. Second, we prove that, despite this lack of compactness at the parameter level, the physical states associated with minimizing sequences converge strongly to the solution of the elliptic problem. Third, under weighted regularity assumptions, we derive an explicit construc-tive convergence rate. Fourth, we show that the same state-level stability mechanism extends to residual minimization formulations. The residual-minimization viewpoint is guided by PDE stability theory. For the elliptic model considered below, the natural residual norm is H−1(Rd), reflecting the fact that −∆ + I is an isomorphism from H1(Rd) to H−1(Rd). Thus, residual functionals should be formulated in spaces consistent with the well-posedness framework of the underlying PDE, rather than through arbitrary pointwise or purely empirical norms. Although the analysis is carried out in a simple elliptic setting, the mechanisms identified here are structural in nature and are expected to extend far beyond the specific model under con-sideration, including variable-coefficient problems, nonlinear equations, bounded domains, and broader classes of neural architectures. Accordingly, the present framework should be viewed primarily as a mathematical test case that makes it possible to isolate and analyze, in a rigorous and transparent manner, phenomena such as the coercivity gap, parameter escape, and state-level convergence. The Gaussian ansatz is therefore not the main object of interest, but rather a convenient analytical vehicle through which these mechanisms can be revealed and understood. We expect analogous effects to arise in a wide range of neural PDE solvers and scientific machine learning methodologies. This creates a challenge of a different nature. Even when the variational behavior of minimizing sequences can be understood theoretically, constructing them numerically remains highly nontrivial. • the possible lack of coercivity, which we will confirm and analyze below; 

• the lack of convergence guarantees of gradient-based methods. In practice, stochastic vari-ants of gradient descent are used to mitigate local minima and reduce computational cost, but they only provide probabilistic guarantees and do not ensure convergence to a global minimizer. 4These facts mark a fundamental structural difference with classical numerical methods, based on linear approximation and Galerkin methods, such as FEM. Despite these difficulties, the analysis developed in this article will explain the good approx-imation properties that these methods exhibit in practice. The key idea is that, rather than at-tempting to track the behavior of the parameters Θ generated by the training process, one should focus on the associated states. As we shall see, the behavior of these states can indeed be under-stood and explained using classical tools from PDE analysis.

More generally, when two neurons condense, difference quotients of activation profiles may generate effective derivative profiles that lie outside the original finite ansatz class. Similar mechanisms are expected in deeper archi-tectures whenever condensation in one layer produces a profile outside the fixed parametrized 5class, and this defect is propagated through subsequent layers. In what follows, however, we keep the Gaussian setting as a transparent model in which the mechanism can be analyzed explicitly. The obstruction is independent of nonconvexity: it persists after relaxation and convexifica-tion and is therefore intrinsic to the geometry of the neural parametrization. As a consequence, gradient-based training may exhibit parameter escape , with parameter sequences diverging while the associated physical states remain bounded and convergent. This reveals a fundamental dis-tinction between parameter space and state space: coercivity may fail in the former while remain-ing fully effective in the latter. In practice, one may therefore obtain accurate approximations of the physical solution even when the underlying parameters become unstable. The next section is devoted to the analysis of this state-level stabilization mechanism.

# 5 Quantitative approximation rates 

We now give a constructive, non-optimal rate for approximating the solution u by Gaussian wave packets. The goal is not to compete with the sharpest results in radial basis function approxi-mation theory [13, 1, 19], but rather to show how explicit rates follow from standard PDE and spectral arguments.

The result above only ensures convergence for 

k sufficiently large, without providing an explicit rate for this dependence. This analysis applies not only to exact minimizing sequences of parameters, but also to near-minimizing sequences. Indeed, assume that, for each P , the parameters under consideration generate states whose asymptotic energy levels remain below IP + ε, for some fixed ε > 0. Then any weak accumulation point u∗ in H1(Rd), as P → ∞ , satisfies 

I ≤ J(u∗) ≤ I + ε. 

By the coercivity of J around its unique minimizer u, this implies 

∥u∗ − u∥H1(Rd) ≤ C√ε. 

Thus, even near-minimizing sequences remain close, at the level of state functions, to the exact solution. In computational practice, however, constructing such minimizing or near-minimizing se-quences is far from straightforward. Once the functional J is restricted to the P -Gaussian ansatz, it becomes a highly nonconvex function of the parameters. Consequently, standard optimization algorithms may converge only to local minima, saddle regions, or ill-conditioned near-critical configurations, and the present argument does not by itself justify convergence of the training dynamics to the absolute minimum.

# 8 Further structural issues and remedies 

The previous sections identify the coercivity gap as a structural obstruction arising from the nonlinear geometry of Gaussian neural parametrizations. We now collect several complemen-tary remarks concerning possible remedies, variants of the ansatz, and the persistence of the phenomenon beyond the model setting considered above. 

Alternative kernels and activation functions 

The loss of coercivity in the relaxed Gaussian formulation is closely related to the strong smooth-ing effect of convolution by G, whose Fourier transform decays exponentially. This suggests asking whether other kernels could improve the parameter-space behavior. One possible modification is to replace G by a periodic Gaussian sum

This restores a better-posed relaxed problem, but at a price: atomic approximations generate periodic Gaussian sums, effectively involving infinitely many translated Gaussians, and an additional truncation step is needed to recover a finite-width ansatz. Another possibility is to use Riesz-type kernels

whose Fourier transform satisfies ̂

For the special choice α = d − 1, the gradient of Hα ∗ μ is controlled through Riesz transforms of μ, so that the homogeneous H1-seminorm of the state is comparable to an L2-type norm of the parameter. However, the low-frequency behavior of the full H1-norm remains delicate. 13 Thus Riesz kernels reduce some of the Gaussian smoothing difficulties but introduce their own analytical complications. These observations reinforce the central message: the stability of a neural or kernel ansatz depends not only on approximation power, but also on how the parameter-to-state map interacts with the PDE energy space. In the remainder of the paper, we return to the Gaussian setting and discuss a more direct regularization mechanism.

From the computational viewpoint, this increases the cost of the method, since the quality of the approximation may depend sensitively on the balance between the PDE energy and the penalization term. From the analytical viewpoint, the presence of δ also adds a further limiting process: one has to understand not only the approximation properties for fixed δ > 0, but also the behavior of minimizers as δ → 0. The techniques developed above apply to this vanishing-regularization regime as well, but the additional parameter makes the convergence analysis more delicate. It is also important to distinguish the relaxed and finite-dimensional problems. While the measure-valued functional Jδr,T is convex in μ, the finite-dimensional penalized functional JδP,T

In particular, the construction of minimizing or near-minimizing parameter sequences remains a challenging computational problem. One may attempt to enforce convexity in the parameter space by taking δ very large. In that regime, however, the penalization dominates the PDE energy and drives the minimizers toward configurations with small total variation rather than toward accurate approximations of the PDE solution. This trade-off in the parameter space between convexity and coercivity on one hand, and approximation accuracy in the other, is one of the main limitations of penalization-based remedies. The limit δ → 0 formally recovers the original unregularized problem, but uniform bounds on the parameters may again be lost in that limit. Hence Tikhonov regularization provides a useful stabilization mechanism for fixed δ > 0, while the vanishing-regularization regime requires the state-level convergence analysis developed in the previous sections. 

Persistence beyond the model setting 

The analysis has been developed for a constant-coefficient elliptic problem and a shallow Gaus-sian ansatz, but the mechanism is structural. Similar compactness defects may arise whenever a 14 nonlinear parametrization admits collisions or degeneracies that generate limiting profiles out-side the fixed ansatz class. This includes variable-coefficient elliptic problems, nonlinear vari-ational equations, bounded domains, and residual formulations (which will be discussed in the next section). The precise form of the pathology depends on the activation and architecture. For smooth activations, condensation often produces derivative profiles through difference quotients. For deeper networks, analogous effects are expected whenever such degeneracies occur in one layer and are transported through the remaining layers.

These variants are consistent with recent convergence analyses of PINNs [17, 14, 4]. As before, the result concerns minimizing or near-minimizing sequences. It does not assert that standard training algorithms necessarily construct them, since the residual loss expressed in neural parameters may contain local minima, flat regions, and ill-conditioned directions. 

# 10 HYCO methodss 

We do not attempt to establish a complete convergence theorem for HYCO methods here. Rather, we aim to identify the main analytical ingredients and illustrate, through a concrete example, how the convergence analysis developed in this paper may be extended to the HYCO framework. The convergence analysis developed above can be extended to more sophisticated frame-works for data-driven PDE modeling, such as the HYCO (hybrid-cooperative learning) methods introduced in [9, 10]. HYCO combines several interacting components—typically a physics-based model and a synthetic, data-driven one—whose parameters are updated in an alternating and cooperative manner. As a consequence, the optimization problem becomes significantly more intricate, involving coupled objectives and interacting sources of approximation and modeling error. Despite this increased complexity, many of the analytical ideas developed in this paper re-main applicable. In particular, the variational and compactness arguments continue to provide useful information on the behavior of the corresponding state functions, even when coercivity fails at the level of the parameters. As in the neural approximation setting studied above, HYCO parametrizations may exhibit concentration phenomena, parameter instabilities, or escape to infinity, so that convergence of the parameters themselves cannot generally be expected. Nevertheless, for canonical linear PDE models, the techniques introduced in the previous sec-tions can be adapted to show that physical states generated by minimizing or near-minimizing HYCO sequences still converge toward the correct solution. As before, these results concern minimizing or approximately minimizing sequences. Whether practical training algorithms ac-tually produce such sequences remains a challenging open problem because of the nonconvex and hybrid nature of the optimization landscape. A detailed analysis of the convergence and optimization properties of HYCO methods is be-yond the scope of this paper and will be addressed elsewhere. Here we restrict ourselves to a prototypical HYCO setting whose purpose is to illustrate how the analytical framework devel-oped above extends to hybrid methods. Our goal is therefore not to establish a complete conver-gence theory, but rather to identify the key mechanisms and analytical ingredients underlying state-level convergence. The results above leave several directions open. 

• Comparison principles and positivity preservation. Another important direction concerns the compatibility of neural ansätze with comparison and maximum-principle properties. For the elliptic problem considered in this paper, the continuous equation sat-isfies the positivity principle 

f ≥ 0 =⇒ u ≥ 0.

Classical discretizations may preserve this property under suitable structural conditions, for instance through discrete maximum principles. By contrast, an unrestricted Gaussian mixture 

uP (x) = 

> P∑
> j=1

wj G(x − xj )

does not, in general, preserve positivity, since the coefficients wj may have arbitrary signs. Moreover, the usual variational argument based on replacing a competitor v by its positive part v+ is not available within the fixed Gaussian manifold: even if v ∈ M P , one generally has v+ /∈ M P . Thus, MP is not stable under truncations, in sharp contrast with the ambient energy space H1(Rd).

One may enforce positivity by restricting to nonnegative weights or by using positivity-preserving parametrizations, but this changes both the approximation class and the associ-ated optimization problem. Understanding whether one can design neural approximation spaces that combine flexibility, approximation power, and compatibility with comparison principles remains an important open problem. This phenomenon provides another man-ifestation of the structural gap between classical variational discretizations and nonlinear neural ansätze. 

• Sharp approximation rates. The logarithmic convergence estimate established here is constructive and conservative. Determining the optimal nonlinear approximation rates for Gaussian neural ansätze in the energy topology remains an important open problem. Such rates should reflect the interplay between the regularity, decay properties, and di-mension of the underlying solution. A particularly promising direction is to develop the present theory within the framework of Barron spaces and related function classes that ad-mit dimension-robust approximation properties (see [2]). This could lead to quantitative convergence estimates that substantially alleviate, or even overcome in suitable regimes, the curse of dimensionality for elliptic PDEs. 

• Algorithms for minimizing sequences. The analysis explains the behavior of minimiz-ing and near-minimizing sequences, but it does not provide an algorithm for constructing them. Designing optimization strategies that retain state-level convergence while avoiding parameter escape is a central challenge. 

• Random features and frozen centers. Freezing the nonlinear parameters turns the Gaussian ansatz into a linear Galerkin-type method. The frozen centers may be chosen randomly or through quantization/Voronoi-type procedures, such as Lloyd’s algorithm [11]. Comparing the stability and convergence of such random-feature methods with fully adaptive nonlinear training is an important bridge between FEM-type discretizations and neural approximation. 18 • Hybrid methods. The mathematical analysis of HYCO methods remains largely open. Beyond the convergence of minimizing sequences, one would like to understand the inter-play between physical and synthetic components, the effect of coupling and regularization parameters, the emergence of concentration phenomena in parameter space, and the extent to which practical alternating-training procedures approximate global minimizers. Devel-oping a rigorous theory for these questions would provide mathematical foundations for a broad class of hybrid approaches to scientific machine learning.

Similarly, second-order derivatives can be approximated by Gaus-sian wave packets involving a finite number of translated Gaussians; the same principle applies to derivatives of arbitrary order. This allows us to approximate the truncated expansion ∑Lℓ=1 πℓv by a linear combination of translated Gaussians. Importantly, one does not need to approximate each term in the sum independently. Rather, the expansion can be reorganized by collecting all derivatives of the same order, so that at each order, one approximates a single linear combination of Gaussian derivatives. Consequently, the number of Gaussian translates required for such an approximation scales as

to achieve an error of order

Equivalently, in terms of the number P of Gaussian wave packets, the error behaves as

This is a linear approximation procedure, and it therefore still suffers from the classical alge-braic curse of dimensionality. Our goal here, however, is to remain as close as possible to classical numerical approximation methods. Up to this point, the analysis has been carried out in weighted Sobolev spaces, and in particular in H1(K). The natural energy space for problem (1), however, is H1(Rd). Bridging this gap requires one additional approximation step, based on a localization argument, which is the purpose of the next subsection.
