Abstract 

Learning chaotic dynamical systems from data requires more than short-term predictive accuracy: the learned model must preserve the geometry of the at-tractor and its invariant statistics. Conventional trajectory (zero-order) and Jacobian (first-order) matching supervise the values and tangent structure of the vector field, but neither constrains how the field bends away from its lo-cal tangent plane. In these approaches, a learned model matches values and tangents at the supervised states, and yet it may curve differently from the truth dynamics. As a result it could be locally accurate while drifting globally toward spurious attractors and distort long-time statistics. We show that en-forcing second-order consistency mitigates these failures. However, forming the full Hessian tensors is computationally prohibitive in high dimensions. We propose model-constrained randomized Jacobian matching , in which the Jacobians of the true and learned vector fields are compared at randomly perturbed inputs. A Taylor expansion analysis shows that the expected ran-domized Jacobian loss decomposes into the nominal Jacobian mismatch plus a Hessian mismatch scaled by the noise variance, implicitly enforcing second-order consistency at O(d2) cost without explicitly forming the O(d3) Hessian tensor. The proposed method uses only Jacobian evaluations, thus is prac-tical in high-dimensional regime where explicit Hessian matching no longer

1. Introduction 

Learning dynamical systems from time-series data has a long history in science and engineering [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. Most existing methods train by min-imizing discrepancies between observed and predicted trajectories [4], or by enforcing governing equations through residual minimization as in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINN) [5, 6]. Although these methods often achieve accurate short-term prediction, they do not explicitly constrain the long-time qualitative behavior of the learned dynamics [7, 8]. In the con-text of dynamical systems, derivative-informed approaches, such as Jaco-bian matching [8, 9], combine trajectory supervision with constraints on the local tangent sensitivities of the learned dynamics. Related ideas ap-pear in gradient-enhanced PINN training [10] and in knowledge distillation via input-Jacobian matching [11, 12]. Park et al. [8] found that Jacobian matching can recover the Lorenz 63 attractor, its invariant measure, and the associated Lyapunov spectrum. Tian [9] also showed that enforcing Jacobian consistency during training improves the accuracy of the tangent linear and adjoint models of a neural-network-emulated Lorenz 96 system, making it suitable for data assimilation. Both trajectory matching and Jacobian matching share a structural lim-itation. Trajectory supervision fixes only the value of the learned vector field, while Jacobian supervision additionally pins down its local tangent plane. Neither, however, constrains how the vector field curves away from that plane, leaving its second-order structure unconstrained. In chaotic systems, the global geometry of the attractor depends on the 2curvature of the vector field. In the Lorenz 63 system, the second derivatives of the vector field are constant throughout phase space, implying that the system’s nonlinear curvature is spatially uniform. The folding structure of the attractor therefore emerges from accumulated nonlinearity along trajectories. Thus, a learned model may reproduce local first-order behavior while still deviating from the true higher-order geometry of the flow. Reproducing chaotic attractors faithfully requires enforcing second-order consistency in addition to conventional first-order matching. The most direct way to enforce curvature consistency is explicit Hes-sian matching, which penalizes discrepancies in second-order derivatives. In practice, however, this approach is often computationally prohibitive; explicit construction of the full Hessian tensor requires O(d3) memory and at least 

O(d2) automatic-differentiation (AD) passes through the vector field for a 

d-dimensional state space. We therefore seek a way to enforce second-order consistency without explicit Hessian construction. The model-constrained (MC) framework in [13, 14] motivates our ap-proach. By evaluating the training loss at randomly perturbed inputs, the MC approach promotes Jacobian consistency without requiring derivative computations: a Taylor expansion shows that the expected squared error between true and learned trajectories at perturbed inputs penalizes the Ja-cobian mismatch to leading order. We propose model-constrained randomized Jacobian matching, which matches Jacobians at perturbed inputs to obtain implicit second-order super-vision. Section 3 shows that minimizing this loss implicitly penalizes Hessian mismatch on average without constructing the Hessian tensor. This makes the approach practical in high-dimensional settings where explicit Hessian matching is infeasible. We test our framework on two chaotic systems: the Lorenz 63 system (low-dimensional chaos with a single positive Lyapunov exponent) and the coupled Lorenz 96 system. The coupled Lorenz 96 system produces high-dimensional chaos in a 396 -dimensional multiscale slow–fast state space with multiple positive Lyapunov exponents. We focus learning on the quadratic advection term of the 36 slow variables, using a structured architecture that encodes spatial locality and translation equivariance. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: 

• We identify a structural limitation of trajectory and Jacobian match-ing: neither constrains the curvature of the vector field. As a result, 3first-order methods can fail catastrophically—under minimal temporal supervision in Lorenz 63, and under out-of-distribution forcing in cou-pled Lorenz 96—while still reproducing the local first-order structure of the true dynamics. 

• We propose model-constrained randomized Jacobian matching, an im-plicit second-order supervision framework that enforces curvature con-sistency at an O(d) AD passes and O(d2) memory cost without explicit construction of the Hessian tensor. 

• A Taylor expansion analysis shows that, to leading order in the noise variance, the randomized Jacobian loss decomposes into a standard Jacobian matching term and an implicit Hessian matching term. 

• For the Lorenz 63 system, we show that second-order supervision recov-ers the curvature of the learned vector field, the Lyapunov spectrum, and the invariant measure of the attractor, whereas first-order methods can fail catastrophically under minimal temporal supervision. 

• For the coupled Lorenz 96 system, we show that second-order super-vision preserves the true invariant statistics and vector-field curvature in strongly chaotic regimes, whereas first-order methods can transi-tion into spurious high-amplitude regimes. These failures persist de-spite architectural inductive biases and are not detected by Lyapunov-spectrum agreement. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the two benchmark systems, Lorenz 63 and coupled Lorenz 96. Section 3 develops the randomized Jacobian matching framework and proves that, to leading order in the perturbation scale, the resulting loss penalizes the Hes-sian mismatch. Section 4 reports the numerical experiments and compares the five supervision methods on both systems. Section 5 concludes. 

2. Model Problems 

We evaluate our approach on two canonical chaotic dynamical systems that span low- and high-dimensional regimes: the Lorenz 63 system [15] and the coupled Lorenz 96 system [16]. 42.1. Lorenz 63 Model 

The Lorenz 63 model is a simplified dynamical system originally intro-duced to model atmospheric convection. The system is governed by 

dx dt = σ (y − x) , (1a) 

dy dt = x (ρ − z) − y, (1b) 

dz dt = xy − βz, (1c) where x, y, and z denote the state variables. Under the standard parameter set σ = 10 , ρ = 28 , and β = 8 /3, the Lorenz 63 system is chaotic and dissipative. Although the system is highly sensitive to initial conditions, making accurate long-term trajectory predic-tion impractical, its ergodicity ensures that time averages along a trajectory converge to statistical averages over the attractor [17]. Using this property, we evaluate the learned models with the invariant measure, the Wasserstein-1 distance [18, 19], and the Lyapunov spectrum ( λ1 > 0, λ2 = 0 , λ3 < 0). The Lyapunov spectrum characterizes the local stability structure: λ1 governs exponential sensitivity to initial conditions, while λ3 reflects dissipation and contraction toward the attractor.

A NODE defines a dynamical system, not merely a sequence predictor. If the learned vector field deviates from the true dynamics, even small pertur-bations in initial conditions can amplify over time, leading to large prediction errors and qualitative failures of the long-term dynamics. To improve robust-ness, we adopt the MC framework, in which losses are evaluated at randomly perturbed states. This framework induces implicit derivative supervision: trajectory matching at perturbed states penalizes first-order (Jacobian) mis-match, and Jacobian matching at perturbed states penalizes second-order (Hessian) mismatch.

4. Numerical Experiments 

We evaluate the five methods of Table 1 on the Lorenz 63 system and the coupled Lorenz 96 system. These methods span a hierarchy of derivative supervision. The naive method performs only zero-order trajectory match-ing. The mc method introduces implicit first-order information through the model-constrained approach. The jac method adds explicit Jacobian super-vision, while hes further incorporates explicit Hessian supervision. Finally, 

mcjac combines Jacobian matching with the model-constrained approach, which by Theorem 1 induces implicit second-order supervision. The methods naturally form two paired comparisons that isolate the role of second-order supervision. The first, mc versus mcjac , tests the same effect in the noise-perturbed setting, where randomized Jacobian matching provides implicit second-order supervision. The second, jac versus hes , examines the effect of adding explicit second-order supervision. The naive method serves as a baseline, illustrating the limitations of trajectory-only supervision for chaotic systems. 12 4.1. Lorenz 63 Model 

The training data were generated by integrating the true Lorenz 63 sys-tem over t ∈ [0 , 500] with time step ∆t = 0 .01 . The system was initialized from 32 initial conditions sampled uniformly from a unit cube centered at 

(x, y, z ) = ( −15 , −15 , 5) . Among these trajectories, 16 were used for training and the remaining 16 for validation. An initial transient of 1,000 time steps was discarded so that all trajectories lie on the attractor. To approximate the vector field bf of the Lorenz 63 system in (4), we employed a fully con-nected neural network R3 → R3 with three hidden layers, each containing 

512 neurons, and tanh activations to ensure smooth derivatives. The neural network was optimized using the Adam optimizer with a constant learning rate of 10 −3 for 10 4 epochs, batch size nb = 512 , and rollout length m = 1 .A single-step segment provides the least amount of temporal information and therefore forces the model to rely almost entirely on the local geometric supervision provided by the Jacobian and Hessian terms. Under ergodicity, a sufficiently large collection of decorrelated single-step pairs (u0, u1) samples the attractor according to its invariant measure [17]. The associated Jaco-bians (J0, J1) and Hessians (H0, H1), being state-dependent quantities, then characterize the local tangent and curvature structure over statistically repre-sentative regions of the attractor. Thus, in the ergodic limit, local matching can constrain global dynamical invariants. The m = 1 regime therefore tests whether local geometric constraints alone can reproduce invariant measures and Lyapunov exponents.

4.2. Lorenz 96 Model 

The Lorenz 63 results show a clear hierarchy of derivative supervision, with randomized Jacobian matching ( mcjac ) matching the performance of explicit Hessian supervision ( hes ). We next consider the coupled Lorenz 96 20 system in (2), a 396 -dimensional multiscale slow–fast system with K = 36 

slow variables and J = 10 fast variables per slow site. At the nominal forcing F = 10 and under larger out-of-distribution forcing values, the system exhibits high-dimensional chaotic dynamics with multiple positive Lyapunov exponents. In this experiment, we adopt a universal differential equation framework [23] in which only a subset of the dynamics is learned. We preserve the known physical structure of the slow equation (2a) and replace only its quadratic nonlinear advection term with a neural network. We consider 

dX k

dt = ˆfk (X; θ) − Xk + F − hY k, (15) where ˆfk(X; θ) parameterizes the quadratic nonlinear advection operator among the slow variables. The linear damping term −Xk, the constant forc-ing F , and the coupling to the true fast variable Y k are kept exact. Thus, the learning task is not to identify the full vector field, but to recover a structured nonlinear component within a strongly chaotic multiscale system. The Hessian of the true quadratic interaction −Xk−1(Xk−2 − Xk+1 ) is sparse, local, and state-independent, with nonzero entries of {− 1, +1 }. The interaction itself is translation-equivariant under cyclic shifts of the slow in-dex k. Exploiting both properties, we take ˆfk to be a shared local multilayer perceptron (MLP), applied identically at every slow site. This locality re-stricts the second-derivative coupling to a sparse banded stencil, reducing the number of structurally nonzero Hessian entries from O(K2) to O((2 W +1) 2).In this study, we choose W = 2 , so that ˆfk = ˆfk (Xk−2, X k−1, X k, X k+1 , X k+2 ; θ),where the local mapping is implemented by an MLP with layer widths 

[5 , 64 , 64 , 1] and tanh activations. This architecture directly embeds the lo-cality and symmetry structure of Lorenz 96 into the neural parameterization, making second-order supervision computationally tractable. A single trajectory is generated from a random initial condition—slow variables perturbed around F , fast variables initialized near zero—by inte-grating for 200 warmup steps ( t = 1 ) to drive the system onto the attractor. We verified that both the slow- and fast-variable energies reach statistically stationary levels within this interval. The resulting trajectory is used as training data. A second trajectory generated from an independently sampled initial condition with the same warmup procedure is used for validation, and all evaluation metrics are computed on this independent validation trajec-tory. Training uses the TSIT5 integrator [24] with timestep ∆t = 0 .005 over 

t ∈ [0 , 500] , batch size nb = 128 , rollout length m = 2 , and 10 ,000 epochs. 21 Unlike the Lorenz 63 experiments, the coupled Lorenz 96 setting required double-precision arithmetic to maintain stable long-horizon integration. Unlike the Lorenz 63 experiments, here the learning problem combines partial learning of a structured nonlinear component with a strongly translation-equivariant architecture. These inductive biases reduce the effective complex-ity of the approximation problem, so within the training regime the perfor-mance gap between supervision methods is small. At the same time, all models are trained at F = 10 and evaluated up to the more chaotic out-of-distribution regime F = 20 . We first compare all five methods at two representative forcing values, F = 10 and F = 20 , including short- and long-time trajectories, Lyapunov spectra, curvature statistics, invariant measures, and Jacobian recovery. 5. Conclusion 

This work investigated the role of derivative supervision in learning chaotic dynamical systems. On both Lorenz 63 and coupled Lorenz 96, we showed that trajectory and Jacobian matching do not constrain the higher-order geometry of the learned vector field. Such models may reproduce short-time trajectories and local tangent behavior while still developing spurious 33 attractors, distorted invariant measures and Lyapunov spectrum, or losing long-time stability. We therefore proposed randomized Jacobian matching, which extends model-constrained training to implicit second-order supervision by matching Jacobians at perturbed states. We proved that the resulting loss contains a leading-order Hessian mismatch penalty without computing Hessian ten-sors. This makes second-order supervision feasible in high dimension. The methods incorporating second-order information—explicitly through Hessian matching or implicitly through randomized Jacobian matching—preserved invariant statistics, Lyapunov spectrum, curvature distributions, and long-term stability. In the high-dimensional Lorenz 96 experiments, randomized Jacobian matching achieved Hessian-level accuracy at first-order cost. Faithful learning of chaotic dynamics requires constraining both the tan-gent dynamics of the flow and its higher-order geometric structure. Two limitations remain. The first is that the coupled Lorenz 96 experiments considered a structured universal differential equation setting in which only the quadratic advection term was learned; extending the approach to fully learned high-dimensional systems, and ultimately to spatiotemporally chaotic partial differential equations, remains future work. The second is that ran-domized Jacobian matching is sensitive to the perturbation scale and loss-weighting parameters, and a systematic study of this dependence would clar-ify the optimal regime for randomized Jacobian matching.
