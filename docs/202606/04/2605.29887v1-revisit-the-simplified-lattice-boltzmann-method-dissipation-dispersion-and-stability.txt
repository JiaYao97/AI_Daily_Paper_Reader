# Revisit the simplified lattice Boltzmann method: dissipation, dispersion and stability 

Zhengwei He a,b , Zhen Chen a,b,вА†

## Abstract 

The simplified lattice Boltzmann method (SLBM) is a recent development in the lattice Boltzmann method (LBM) community, addressing the intrinsic limitations of the traditional LBM by directly evolving macroscopic quantities and maintaining numerical stability in high-Reynolds-number simulations. However, fundamental understanding of the numerical dissipation and dispersion of SLBM is still lacking, and the origin of its good numerical stability remains unknown. In this work, a generalized formulation is developed, revealing that the SLBM recovers modified macroscopic equations containing both intrinsic physical deviations and numerical truncation errors. To remove these deviations, the macroscopic equation derived from the standard BGK-LBM is adopted as a reference model and solved by the predictorвАУcorrector strategy, which constitutes the reformulated SLBM. The proposed method uses the generalized SLBM formulation in the predictor step with tunable high-order parameters, while the corrector step is realized by the finite-difference discretization. Chen) 2controlling numerical dissipation and dispersion, which is then validated in more complicated numerical examples. It is demonstrated that the reformulated SLBM preserves the second-order accuracy, improves the dispersionвАУdissipation performance, enhances numerical stability, and resolves fine vortex structures on relatively coarse grids. Thus, the proposed method combines improved numerical properties with the simplicity of SLBM, offering a high-fidelity and stable scheme for incompressible flow simulations. 

Key words: Simplified lattice Boltzmann method; dissipation; dispersion; numerical stability. 

## 1 Introduction 

The lattice Boltzmann method (LBM) [1вАУ3] is a mesoscopic numerical approach for simulating flow problems [4вАУ8]. Over decades of development, the numerical characteristics of LBM have been well recognized, and its popularity in the computational mechanics community has been growing due to its kinetic nature, simplicity, and explicitness. However, intrinsic limitations such as high cost of virtual memory and inconvenient implementation of physical boundary conditions remain. In addition, the standard single-relaxation-time (SRT) LBM can be unstable at low lattice viscosity, which can be alleviated by the multiple-relaxation-time (MRT) schemes [9], the entropic LBM (ELBM) [10] and others. The simplified lattice Boltzmann method (SLBM) [11] was proposed as a robust alternative to resolve the aforementioned limitations of the standard LBM. Unlike the standard LBM, SLBM directly evolves macroscopic quantities using the predictor-corrector strategy, which circumvents the storage of distribution functions, reduces the memory consumption and improves numerical stability [12,13]. Customized solvers were then devised in the SLBM framework to tackle multiple 3types of flow problems including thermal flows [14,15], multiphase flows [16], phase-change modeling [17], and non-Newtonian fluids [18]. In the meantime, to accommodate complex geometries, SLBM has been integrated with non-uniform grids [19] and immersed boundary methods [20]. Furthermore, owing to its numerical stability and memory-efficient structure, SLBM has been extended to a broad range of physical problems. Representative applications include magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) [21], ferrofluid droplet deformation [22], nanofluid convection [23], and GPU-accelerated implementations [24]. Over the past decade, algorithmic developments of the SLBM have followed two complementary directions: incorporating additional physics and refining the time-marching procedure to improve efficiency. On the physical side, Dai et al. [25] introduced an enthalpy-based version (SELBM) with an entropy-based source term for phase-change simulations [26], while Gao et al. [27] reformulated the equilibrium distribution to achieve a strictly incompressible formulation (IC-SHSLBM). Concurrently, efforts to streamline time integration have produced a family of single-step methods, including the SS-SLBM for turbulence proposed by Delgado-Guti√©rrez et al. [28] and its extension to 3D MHD [29], as well as the one-step methos (OSLBM) proposed by Qin et al. [30,31] and its improved version NOSLBM for large-density-ratio multiphase flows [32]. Despite its well-documented numerical stability presented by the above works and Ref. [12,13,33], numerical intuition suggests that the improved stability could come at the cost of extra dissipation. Some practices have confirmed this, and techniques like local mesh refinement [34,35] or high-order interpolation (HSLBM [36]) are required to recover delicate flow structures on comparable mesh sizes. Several attempts have been made to restore this trade-off. Chen et al. [37] reduced numerical dissipation by reformulating the predictor step, though at the expense of 4stability. Gao et al. [38] proposed a consistent forcing scheme, in which the predictor-corrector equations are reconstructed based on the GZS forcing model in LBM [39]. This enables SLBM to interpret external forcing effects with better accuracy, especially in the circumstances with non-uniform forcing fields. They further introduced an additional correction step [40] (CSLBM) to enhance accuracy. In the context of multiphase flow simulations, Li et al. [41] modified the phase-field solver by solving the AllenвАУCahn equation instead of the CahnвАУHilliard equation in Ref. [16], which improves the interfacial sharpness. While these approaches differ in implementation, one important insight emerges: numerical performance of the SLBM are fundamentally associated with the macroscopic equations it reconstructs. This viewpoint was further supported by Lu et al. [42], who showed that the SLBM essentially reconstructs solutions to the macroscopic equations with additional terms responsible for its strong numerical stability. These investigations suggest that improving SLBM accuracy without extra mesh refinement or high-order techniques requires a deeper understanding of the SLBMвАЩs equivalent macroscopic equations and their numerical implications. In this paper, we present a generalized formulation that enables a more comprehensive theoretical analysis of the SLBM by recasting it as a specific reconstruction of the macroscopic equations. Using macroscopic equations derived from the BGK-LBM [43] as a reference baseline, we show that the SLBM solves the equations containing both intrinsic physical deviations and numerical truncation errors. Motivated by this finding, we replace the macroscopic equations reconstructed by the original SLBM with the physically consistent reference model and propose a new predictorвАУcorrector scheme. The predictor step is reconstructed from equilibrium moments via the generalized SLBM, while the corrector step employs the finite-difference discretization. The equilibrium-based reconstruction introduces tunable parameters associated with high-order 5derivatives in the macroscopic equations, allowing flexible adjustments of numerical dissipation, dispersion, and stability. Systematic linear wave analysis and numerical benchmarks demonstrate the advantages of the present method over the original SLBM and validate the dissipation inherent in the original SLBM. In general, the proposed method establishes an algorithmic protocol that allows constructing well-balanced schemes in the SLBM framework, with which low memory cost, convenient boundary treatment and good numerical stability are maintained, and the numerical dissipation is largely reduced. The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the principles of the SLBM with theoretical analysis and derives the proposed method with mathematical details. Section 3 presents a carefully designed linear wave analysis to offer a semi-quantitative understanding of the numerical dissipation and dispersion characteristics of both the original SLBM and the proposed method. Section 4 validates the linear wave analysis through numerical benchmarks and demonstrates the performance of the new method. Finally, conclusions are drawn in Section 5.

2.1.2 The generalized SLBM formulation and theoretical analysis 

While the original SLBM formulations reviewed in Section 2.1.1 offer notable advantages in terms of algorithmic simplicity and memory efficiency, deeper theoretical understanding is required to clarify the origin of its numerical performance. In particular, although the SLBM has demonstrated favorable numerical robustness in practice, its underlying macroscopic consistency and truncation-error structure remain insufficiently understood. To address this issue in a systematic manner, we extend the SLBM to a more generalized formulation and perform a detailed theoretical analysis. The starting point of this analysis is the reconstruction method inherent to the SLBM, namely that macroscopic evolution equations are implicitly synthesized through moments of equilibrium distribution functions. By abstracting away from the specific coefficient choices and mesh node stencil of the original SLBM, this idea leads to the generalized SLBM, in which a broad class of numerical schemes can be expressed within the predictorвАУcorrector structure. Since the predictor step involves only equilibrium distribution functions, the RSLBM still avoids storing distribution functions and therefore retains a memory footprint comparable to that of the original SLBM. This equilibrium-based reconstruction further enables direct control of contributions from higher-order derivatives through the parameters ЁРађЈ and ЁРађЄ , which, as will be demonstrated in Sections 3 and 4, play a central role in controlling numerical dissipation, dispersion, and stability. Boundary conditions are imposed directly on macroscopic variables as in the original SLBM [12].However, near-boundary mesh nodes 22 require special treatments due to stencil reduction, and the adopted strategy together with its numerical implications will be discussed in Section 4.3. 

## 3 Linear wave analysis 

In this section, following the approach of Lallemand et al. [9], the linear wave analysis is adopted to provide a more transparent assessment of numerical dissipation and dispersion. It should be emphasized that this analysis is not intended to replace a complete stability analysis, but rather to offer a focused and semi-quantitative characterization of numerical dissipation and dispersion in controlled conditions. For clarity, the analysis is restricted to one-dimensional setting. The analysis focuses on the predictor step, while the corrector step is treated using the finite-difference discretization described previously. The results elucidate the numerical origins of the strong dissipation observed in the original SLBM and clarify the role of tunable high-order terms in the present reformulated SLBM. These observations will be further examined and validated through numerical benchmarks in Section 4.

3.3 Summary of linear wave analysis 

The linear wave analysis reveals two key mechanisms governing the numerical behavior of the present reformulated SLBM. First, numerical dispersion is dominated by the third-order derivative term associated with ЁРађЈ , which introduces phase errors and reproduces the dispersion behavior of the original SLBM when ЁРађЈ = вИТ1/6 . ## 5 Conclusion 

This work establishes a comprehensive understanding of the numerical dissipation, dispersion, and stability properties of the simplified lattice Boltzmann method (SLBM). By introducing a generalized SLBM formulation, it is shown that the macroscopic equations recovered by the SLBM contain intrinsic physical deviations and numerical truncation errors, which, for the first time, explains the numerical limitations of the SLBM exhibited in practical simulations. 54 To address these limitations, the reformulated SLBM (RSLBM) is proposed. The RSLBM utilizes the more actual macroscopic equation (MAME) as the target macroscopic equations to be recovered and thus successfully removes the physical deviations in the original SLBM. The predictorвАУcorrector strategy is used to reconstruct macroscopic solutions to the target equations. The predictor step is constructed within the generalized SLBM framework with explicitly controllable high-order parameters ЁРађЈ and ЁРађЄ , while the corrector step is based on the finite-difference discretization. Linear wave analysis reveals the distinct contributions of these parameters. The third-order derivative term governed by ЁРађЈ dominates the numerical dispersion and, in the presence of background flow, introduces additional velocity-dependent numerical dissipation, whereas the fourth-order derivative term associated with ЁРађЄ determines the baseline dissipation level and stability characteristics. These results provide a physically motivated and practically useful guideline for the parameter selection of the RSLBM. Numerical experiments are then conducted to validate the analysis. The TaylorвАУGreen vortex demonstrates accuracy and convergence comparable to the finite-difference schemes. The double shear layer problem confirms the linear wave predictions: the RSLBM shows reduced dispersion and dissipation errors and enhanced stability, with high-order derivative terms providing additional stabilization. The lid-driven cavity flow further illustrates the capability of the RSLBM in capturing delicate vortical structures on relatively coarse grids while maintaining numerical stability. In summary, the RSLBM retains the simplicity and memory efficiency of the original SLBM while significantly reducing its dissipation and dispersion. The free parameters in the RSLBM allow flexible adjustment of the magnitude of the high-order derivative terms and thus contribute to fine tuning its numerical performance without sacrificing accuracy. Future work will be focused 55 on adaptive parameter selection based on local flow conditions, extensions to complex geometries through hybrid formulations, and applications to mesh refinement strategies.
