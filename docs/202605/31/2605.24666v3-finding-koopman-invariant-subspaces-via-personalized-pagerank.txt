## Abstract 

Selecting a finite dictionary of observables whose span is Koopman-invariant is a central challenge in data-driven Koopman operator approximation. We address this problem by exploiting zero-block structure in Extended Dynamic Mode De-composition (EDMD) matrices. We show that any sub-dictionary whose span is Koopman-invariant induces an exact zero block in the EDMD matrix, even for finite data. We then show that such blocks can be detected by applying PageRank to a row-normalized EDMD matrix constructed from a large initial dictionary. The the-ory extends to approximately invariant subspaces and yields stronger guarantees for personalized PageRank (PPR) when the seed observables lie inside the target block and reach all observables in that block. Combining EDMD concentration bounds with PageRank perturbation theory gives end-to-end detection guarantees with 

O(1 /‚ąöM ) finite-sample scaling and explicit constants. More generally, without assuming an invariant subspace exists, high PPR mass on a sub-dictionary controls discounted multi-step leakage from the seed observables. Numerical experiments on the Duffing oscillator, Van der Pol oscillator, Lorenz system, and a three-well Ramachandran potential suggest that the method identifies compact, interpretable dictionaries with accurate predictions. 

## 1 Introduction 

Predicting long-term behavior of a nonlinear dynamical system xk+1 = F(xk) is fundamentally difficult: trajectories can exhibit sensitive dependence on initial conditions, complex bifurcation structures, and chaotic attractors. The Koopman operator [ 1, 2] offers an alternative perspective by shifting attention from the state x ‚ąą M ‚äā Rd to observables , i.e., scalar-valued functions 

f : M ‚Üí C. The operator K acts by Kf = f ‚ó¶ F, transforming the nonlinear state-space dynamics into a linear evolution in a function space. The price paid for linearity is infinite-dimensionality, but finite-dimensional projections of K still capture physically meaningful dynamical features. Its growing adoption sparked by [ 3 , 4] is reflected in the coinage ‚ÄúKoopmanism‚ÄĚ [ 5] and thousands of articles over the last decade (see [ 6, 7, 8 , 9, 10 ] for comprehensive reviews). Some popular applications span fluid dynamics [ 6], system identification [ 11 ], neuroscience [ 12 ], and molecular dynamics [13].

Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (EDMD) [ 14 ] approximates K by projecting onto a finite 

dictionary of observables D ňúN = {Ōą1, . . . , Ōą ňúN }, yielding an ňúN √ó ňúN matrix K ňúN ,M estimated from 

M data pairs (x(i), y(i))i=1 ,...,M via least squares where y(i) = F(x(i)). While several convergence results are now well understood [ 15 , 16 , 17 , 18 ], these guarantees depend on the choice of dictionary. In addition, the choice of dictionary strongly affects performance of the approximation [ 9, 19 ]. This raises a central practical question: which observables should the dictionary contain? 

The dictionary learning problem. A natural strategy is to start with a large initial dictionary, for example, all polynomials up to a certain degree, or a dictionary of radial basis functions (RBFs), and hope that they capture relevant dynamics. However, using a large dictionary introduces several difficulties. First, when ňúN is large relative to M , the EDMD matrix can be poorly conditioned, leading to unstable predictions. Second, a large dictionary obscures interpretability: the practitioner cannot tell which observables are relevant and which are harmful. Third, and most fundamentally, EDMD accuracy is not monotone in dictionary size due to spectral pollution [9, 18]. These difficulties motivate the question: given a large initial dictionary D ňúN , how can we select a small sub-dictionary DN ‚äā D ňúN that captures essential dynamics? The ideal sub-dictionary would span a Koopman invariant subspace (i.e., a subspace closed under K) so that the EDMD matrix on this subspace exactly represents the dynamics without leakage to the complementary observables. 

Our approach. We propose an algorithm that selects a sub-dictionary by exploiting a zero block structure of an EDMD matrix. This zero block can be efficiently detected by applying the PageRank (PR) algorithm to the row-normalized EDMD matrix, which reinterprets the matrix as a Markov chain on observables and identifies those forming a ‚Äúclosed community.‚ÄĚ We show that personalized PR 

(PPR), seeded at observables whose prediction matters most, achieves stronger detection guarantees by eliminating the bias-variance tradeoff inherent in standard PR‚Äôs uniform teleportation. Because the initial dictionary consists of observables with known functional forms (e.g., polynomials, RBFs), the selected sub-dictionary yields an explicit, interpretable lifted dynamical system. 

Contributions. 

1. Zero block structure and invariant subspaces. We show that any sub-dictionary spanning a Koopman invariant subspace produces an exact zero block in the EDMD matrix, even with finitely many data (Theorem 2). When a spanned subspace is not exactly invariant, the norm of the off-diagonal block serves as a computable surrogate for prediction error (Propositions 1, 7). 2. PR/PPR-based detection. To detect zero blocks, we propose Algorithm 1 that reinterprets the row-normalized EDMD matrix as a Markov chain on observables and ranks them via PR or PPR. For an exact zero block, PPR succeeds at any damping factor őĪ ‚ąą (0 , 1) , whereas standard PR requires a mixing condition on the internal block (Theorem 3). For an approximate zero block, PPR still succeeds with a range of a damping factor broader than standard PR. 3. Finite-sample guarantees. Combining the perturbation theory with the EDMD matrix concen-tration bounds yields end-to-end detection guarantees at rate O(1 /‚ąöM ) with explicit constants (Theorem 4). The resulting sample-complexity bounds for both PR and PPR show that PPR requires fewer samples than PR (Corollary 4.1). 4. Unconditional leakage bounds. Even without assuming an invariant subspace exists, we show that multi-step prediction error of any selected sub-dictionary is bounded by how much PPR mass falls outside that sub-dictionary (Proposition 9). This gives a principled justification for PPR-based selection purely in terms of prediction accuracy, without any block-structure assumption. 

1.1 Related work Koopman operator theory and dictionary learning. Dictionary choices range from classical bases (polynomials or RBFs [ 14 ]), kernelized EDMD [ 14 , 20 ], time-delay embeddings [ 21 ], to neural-network-learned dictionaries [ 22 , 23 , 24 , 25 , 26 , 27 ]. These approaches focus on constructing expressive dictionaries but do not address sub-dictionary selection from a given large dictionary. Conradie et al. [ 28 ] used a principal angle decomposition for compressing an initial dictionary, selecting modes whose Koopman image remains approximately within the subspace. They also derived error bounds for selected modes, provided a selection criterion, and used multistep bounds 2to train a dictionary. Haseli and Cort√©s [ 29 , 30 ] proposed a subspace decomposition for iterative dictionary pruning, but provides no sample complexity guarantees. In contrast, our method selects an explicit subset of the original dictionary rather than a compressed linear combination of modes, and it provides perturbation and finite-sample guarantees for the resulting selection rule. Our PPR-based sub-dictionary selection criterion has a principled justification through Markov chain theory. A complementary line of work approximates the Koopman (or transfer) operator directly by a 

stochastic matrix . Classical Ulam-type discretizations of the Perron‚ÄďFrobenius operator partition the state space and estimate transition probabilities between cells, yielding a row-stochastic matrix whose stationary and spectral properties approximate those of the transfer operator [ 31 , 32 , 33 ]. Transition matrices in our work can be viewed as an EDMD-level analogue of this construction: instead of discretizing the state space, we row-normalize (the transpose of) the learned EDMD matrix to obtain a Markov chain on observables whose PPR structure encodes sub-dictionary selection. 

PageRank, block structure detection, and spectral methods. The PR algorithm [ 34 ] computes stationary distributions of regularized Markov chains [ 35 ], with well-studied perturbation theory [ 36 ,37 ]. Our perturbation analysis builds on the theory of nearly completely decomposableMarkov chains [ 38 , 39 ]. PPR, introduced by Haveliwala [ 40 ], was connected to local graph partitioning by Andersen, Chung, and Lang [ 41 ]. Block structure detection relates to spectral clustering [ 42 ], community detection [ 43 ], and Perron cluster analysis (PCCA/PCCA+) [ 44 , 45 ]; Lai et al. [ 46 ] studied PR for block detection in stochastic matrices. Our approach differs in starting from EDMD matrices (requiring row normalization and its perturbation analysis) and targeting Koopman invariant subspaces rather than communities or metastable states. Classical invariant subspace algorithms [ 47 , 48 ] do not exploit the specific block structure that Theorem 2 guarantees.

## 5 Conclusions 

We developed a framework connecting zero block structures in EDMD matrices to Koopman invariant subspaces, and proposed Algorithm 1, which applies PPR to a row-normalized EDMD matrix to select sub-dictionaries. The off-diagonal block norm controls prediction accuracy (Propositions 1 and 7), and exact invariant subspaces produce exact zero blocks with finitely many data points (Theorem 2). PPR detects these blocks at any damping factor (Theorem 3) and achieves end-to-end detection at rate O(1 /‚ąöM ) with sample-complexity bounds for both PR and PPR (Theorem 4, Corollary 4.1). More broadly, the multi-step Koopman leakage is bounded by the PPR mass outside the selected set 9(Proposition 9), justifying the method without assuming invariant subspace existence: PPR returns the sub-dictionary that best closes the dynamics around those seeds. Experiments on the Duffing and Van der Pol oscillators, the Lorenz system, and the three-well Ramachandran potential confirm that PPR identifies compact, interpretable dictionaries, reducing one-step prediction error by up to ‚ąľ 70% over random selection at a small sub-dictionary size N (see Table 1). Future directions include constructing invariant structures beyond subsets of a prescribed dictionary and adaptive selection of the damping factor őĪ. Although the experiments indicate that our algorithm performs well for ergodic trajectory data (i.e., the Lorenz system) and stochastic systems (i.e., the Ramachandran potential), our deterministic theory assumes noise-free i.i.d. samples. Extension of the theory to ergodic trajectory data [ 17 , 52 ] and to stochastic systems, where the Koopman operator measures expectations of observables and variance becomes an independent source of error, is an important open direction; the stochastic ResDMD framework of Colbrook et al. [53] provides a natural starting point for such an extension.

A.2 The Koopman operator 

The Koopman operator framework, introduced by Koopman [ 1] and extended by Koopman and von Neumann [ 2], lifts a nonlinear dynamical system on the state space M to a linear (but infinite-dimensional) operator on a function space of observables . This linearization is the foundation of modern data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamics: dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) [ 54 ,55 , 56 ] and its extensions [ 14 , 33 , 57 ] approximate the Koopman operator from trajectory data, and its spectral theory underpins mode decomposition, long-time forecasting, coherent-structure discovery, and reduced-order modeling across fluid mechanics, molecular dynamics, power systems, and control [6, 19, 49, 53]. A.4 Personalized PageRank 

PageRank (PR) was introduced by Page and Brin [ 34 ] to rank web pages by modeling a random surfer on the hyperlink graph who occasionally teleports to a uniformly random page. D.2 Compared methods 

For the two oscillator examples, we compare four dictionary ordering methods alongside the tunable symmetric subspace decomposition (TSSD) baseline. Unordered uses a uniformly random permuta-tion of all ňúN observables as a baseline, so that the first N selected observables carry no structural information, except that the state variables are always included in the sub-dictionary. Incremental 

selects the first N observables in lexicographic order of total degree, i.e., the natural ordering of the Laguerre polynomial basis from lowest to highest degree. PageRank (PR) and Personalized PageRank (PPR) apply Algorithm 1 to the full EDMD matrix K ňúN ,M : the ňúN observables are ranked by their PR or PPR scores, and the top-N are selected to form the sub-dictionary DN . For PPR, the seed set is taken to be the state-coordinate observables. 

TSSD [ 29 ] iteratively prunes the dictionary by retaining only directions that are approximately invariant under both the forward and backward EDMD operators; the tolerance parameter ŌĶ ‚ąą [0 , 1] 

controls how strictly invariance is enforced, with ŌĶ = 0 recovering exact symmetric subspace decomposition (SSD). We sweep ŌĶ over a uniform grid of [0 , 1] with spacing 0.01 and report each detected sub-dictionary size as a scatter point in the error plots. Despite the fine grid, TSSD identifies only a small number of distinct sizes: three for the Duffing oscillator ( N = 3 , 45 , 65 ; Fig. 2a) and seven for the Van der Pol oscillator ( N = 3 , 24 , 25 , 26 , 27 , 28 , 51 ; Fig. 6a), reflecting the coarse granularity of the invariance structure detected by the algorithm.

D.7 Computational resources 

All experiments were conducted on a standard laptop computer equipped with an Apple M5 CPU and 32 GB of RAM. No GPU acceleration was used. All code is implemented in Python, using 

numpy for linear algebra and numba for JIT-compiled simulation of the Ramachandran potential; the overdamped Langevin time-stepping loop is compiled to native machine code via LLVM at the first call, with a short warmup run performed before production to amortize the compilation cost. The total compute time for all experiments reported in the paper is estimated at approximately 30‚Äď40 minutes on the hardware described above.
