Abstract 

Designing e ff ective optimisation strategies for unsteady flows in the presence of complex dynamics is chal-lenging. Gradient-based optimisation algorithms that rely on gradient information obtained from adjoint equations are e ffi cient for high-dimensional control problems such as those considered here. However, they can be prone to numerical sensitivities when the underlying physics is complex, i.e. when it is highly nonlin-ear, non-di ff erentiable and chaotic. This work proposes an ensemble-variational (EnVar) framework, which provides a non-intrusive alternative to classical, adjoint-based approaches for flow control applications. This framework approximates cost-function gradients through a finite ensemble of perturbed control vec-tors. A formulation based on a finite-di ff erence approximation in the ensemble space is employed to address high-dimensional parameter spaces. The methodology is evaluated on two-dimensional cavity flows across Reynolds regimes spanning quasi-periodic to chaotic dynamics, where a steady forcing is optimised. In the quasi-periodic regime, the method identifies control strategies consistent with adjoint-based optimization and achieves a significant reduction of kinetic energy fluctuations, driving the flow toward a periodic limit cycle. In the chaotic regime, the framework remains e ff ective in estimating gradients and mitigating flow fluctuations in situations where adjoint-based approaches typically exhibit convergence issues. This work demonstrates that the EnVar method serves as a computationally e ffi cient, parallelizable, and non-intrusive alternative for high-dimensional optimization problems in complex fluid dynamic regimes. 

Keywords: optimisation, flow control, instability, variational methods 

1. Introduction 

The prediction and control of unsteady flows with complex dynamics remain central challenges in fluid mechanics, particularly when strong instabilities and nonlinear interactions dominate the system response. Flow instabilities can a ff ect many engineering systems, reducing e ffi ciency and making their operation less reliable. This calls for systematic methods to design control strategies. In complex regimes, the design of e ff ective control strategies typically leads to high-dimensional optimisation problems. Optimization methods based on descent directions iteratively minimise a cost function by exploiting gradient information [1]. When analytical derivatives are available, gradients can be evaluated directly at low computational cost, however, in most practical application, analytical gradients are not accessible, motivating the use of alternative numerical approaches. A straightforward approach to gradient approximation is the use of finite-di ff erence (FD) methods. While simple to implement, these methods scale poorly with the number of parameters, as each additional dimension requires an extra function evaluation. Consequently, FD methods 

> arXiv:2605.23812v1 [physics.flu-dyn] 22 May 2026

are generally unsuitable for high-dimensional optimization problems. Similarly, stochastic optimization methods that estimate gradients approximately [2] face significant challenges in high-dimensional spaces. Population-based algorithms, such as genetic algorithms and particle swarm optimization, are capable of exploring complex design spaces; however, they typically require a large number of function evaluations per generation, which makes them computationally prohibitive for high-fidelity CFD simulations. These considerations have led traditional gradient-based optimization methods in CFD to rely on adjoint solvers [3], which enable the e ffi cient computation of sensitivities of a cost function with respect to a large number of control variables. First introduced for ordinary di ff erential equations by Lions [4] and later extended to fluid mechanics by Pironneau [5] and Jameson [6], an adjoint solver enables gradient computation at a cost independent of the number of design parameters. Although highly accurate, adjoint methods can be di ffi cult to implement for complex codes, sensitive to numerical errors, and prone to instability in strongly nonlinear or turbulent flows [7]. In addition, adjoint methods, to o ff er precise gradient information, necessitate an adjoint solver and the storage of the full forward trajectory for unsteady problems, while maintaining the accuracy of the forward–adjoint relationship, becomes challenging for long integration times [8], limiting their applicability in practice. To address the limitations of the aforementioned optimization approaches while preserving computa-tional tractability and broad applicability, ensemble-based variational (EnVar) methods have recently gained attention. These methods provide a non-intrusive alternative by approximating cost-function gradients through a finite ensemble of perturbed control vectors [9, 10]. By projecting the optimization problem onto the subspace spanned by the ensemble, EnVar methods are able to capture nonlinear interactions while keeping the computational cost manageable. Building on the ensemble Kalman methodology introduced by Evensen [11], EnVar techniques have been extended to variational and nonlinear least-squares formu-lations [12, 13, 14, 15] and have seen increasing use in fluid mechanics. Applications include identifying transition-triggering disturbances in hypersonic boundary layers [16], designing optimal thermal actuation for transition delay [17], solving inverse problems in transitional flows [18], shape optimisation in unsteady or stochastic environments where adjoint methods are impractical [19], and LES-based optimisation to sup-press turbulent wake fluctuations under chaotic dynamics [20]. Further developments include passive and active control of vortex shedding [21], nonlinear nonmodal optimisation and droplet-impact control [22], turbulence-model data assimilation [23], and scalar-source identification in turbulent flows [24]. These studies highlight the broad potential of EnVar approaches for tackling nonlinear optimisation problems in fluid mechanics. Despite this progress, a systematic EnVar formulation for high-dimensional unsteady flow control, as well as comparisons between EnVar-based optimisation and established methods such as adjoint-based optimisation, remain underdeveloped. Here, high-dimensional means that the control is not described by a small set of prescribed actuation parameters. Instead, the actuator can be a spatially distributed forcing field, free to vary over the domain and discretized with a large number of degrees of freedom, similarly to a flow variable. By building a physically motivated background covariance, the ensemble subspace is defined and the optimisation is made possible in a reduced-order space. Within this context, the present study develops and assesses an ensemble-variational framework, formulated within an ensemble finite-di ff erence setting [25], for flow optimisation in open-cavity configurations. This formulation provides a more systematic optimisation methodology, improving well-posedness and iterative behaviour. The open cavity represents a canonical separated-flow problem in which shear-layer instabilities can lead to self-sustained oscillations and, at higher Reynolds numbers, chaotic dynamics. The method enables the determination of optimal open-loop control strategies in large control spaces, with the aim of mitigating flow fluctuations. 2. Flow configuration 

This study focuses on the flow over a two-dimensional open cavity [26], a canonical configuration that illustrates fundamental instability mechanisms and has attracted significant attention for both theoretical and applied research. In many practical flow-control problems, strong hydrodynamic instabilities amplify per-turbations and induce unsteady behaviour, often associated with flow separation caused by adverse pressure gradients or geometric discontinuities [27, 28, 29]. In cavity flows, this results in a free shear layer spanning the opening, which is highly susceptible to Kelvin–Helmholtz instabilities [30, 31, 32, 33], leading to vor-tex roll-up, large-scale coherent structures, and strongly unsteady dynamics. At su ffi ciently high Reynolds numbers, the shear layer destabilizes, generating self-sustained oscillations, quasi-periodic limit cycles, or even chaotic behaviour, while recirculating motion develops inside the cavity. Owing to these characteristic dynamics, cavity flows provide an ideal problem for studying instability mechanisms relevant to a wide range of more complex fluid systems. In [34], the mechanisms driving three-dimensional instabilities in open cavity flows are investigated, identifying the critical conditions under which the flow undergoes its first bifurcation, transitioning from a steady two-dimensional state to a steady three-dimensional configura-tion. The influence of three-dimensional e ff ects on the transition to unsteadiness is examined in [35] using linear stability analyses tools. The control of these instabilities has been assessed through many techniques such as model-based feedback, open-loop forcing near Hopf bifurcations, and mean-flow resolvent-based linear models have been successfully applied to stabilize oscillations and suppress quasi-periodic attractors [26, 36, 37, 38, 39, 40]. These studies enable the identification of control strategies in two-dimensional flows, particularly in regimes where linear stability provides meaningful guidance for control. However, the development of control strategies based on numerical models of the nonlinear Navier-Stokes equations, including regimes that may become chaotic, remains an active area of research. 3. Optimisation methodology 

This study compares two methodologies for flow control optimisation: the first approach is based on the adjoint-based optimisation framework, which enables the computation of the exact gradient of a cost func-tional with respect to the control parameters at each iteration. This gradient information is then used within a descent algorithm to iteratively converge toward the optimal solution. The second method uses ensemble-based variational techniques (EnVar) to provide an approximation of the gradient using an ensemble of 4function evaluations. A key advantage of this approach is that it is non-intrusive; it does not require any modification to the computational fluid dynamics (CFD) solver, which makes it easy to implement. Before introducing each method in greater detail, the general form of the optimisation problem of inter-est is presented. In particular, the iterative procedure may be terminated when the decrease in the cost function becomes smaller than a prescribed tolerance, when the norm of the reduced gradient falls below a given threshold, when the update

This formulation en-sures the well-posedness of the problem by providing a balanced weighting between the two components of the cost function. The weighting is automatically determined by the algorithm, and if the observation operator is linear, the method converges to the minimum in a single iteration. This contrasts with classical EnVar approaches, which either use a fixed penalisation parameter for the weights or determine it adaptively during the optimisation process. In addition, the present formulation does not require a convex definition of the cost function, unlike traditional EnVar methods. Finally, accurate directional derivatives can only be achieved using the finite-di ff erence gradient computation if the ensemble members are very close to each other. Classical EnVar methods also use nearby ensemble members for gradient estimation, but the constraints are not as tight as in the present approach. 4. Numerical methods 

The steady, unsteady, and adjoint equations are solved using the finite element method. All equations considered in this study are first reformulated in variational form and then spatially discretized on a triangu-lar mesh. The triangulation is generated using the open-source software FreeFem ++ [43]. The velocity and pressure fields ( q = [u, v, p]) are discretized with Taylor–Hood elements ( P2, P2, P1), employing quadratic 

P2 basis functions for velocity components and linear P1 functions for pressure. All discrete matrices, ob-tained by projecting the variational formulations onto the finite element basis, are assembled in FreeFem ++ 

using a domain-decomposition parallelization strategy and interfaced with the PETSc library for the solution of large sparse linear systems. The matrices are sparse, and their inverses are computed with the UMFPACK library, which provides a sparse direct LU solver. It should be noted that, in practice, the number of adjoint iterations can also depend on the specific optimisation algorithm used, which may include additional line-search or step-size evaluations. An important distinction between the two approaches concerns their parallel e ffi ciency. The ensemble members in the EnVar algorithm are independent and can be executed simultaneously on parallel hard-ware, reducing the wall-clock time of an iteration to nearly that of a single direct evaluation when su ffi cient computational resources are available. This property makes the method highly competitive, particularly for unsteady-flow optimisation problems. The adjoint method is less straightforward to parallelize, since the backward integration depends on the forward trajectory and often requires specific strategies for data stor-age and access. Parallel-in-time adjoint-based optimisation algorithms have been proposed [49], showing that, under suitable assumptions on memory availability and time partitioning, a significant reduction in computational cost can be achieved, up to 70% of the total time of the optimization process. The e ffi ciency of such approaches, however, depends on the chosen time horizon, the communication overhead between processors, and the overall software implementation. In contrast, the EnVar approach relies on the inde-pendent propagation of ensemble members, which allows a direct exploitation of parallel computations and leads to a cost that scales more naturally with available resources. Overall, when measured in terms of direct-equivalent cost, the adjoint method may require fewer simu-lations per iteration, but the intrinsic parallelism of EnVar can lead to substantially lower wall-clock times, making it an attractive alternative in high-performance computing environments. 

6. Conclusions and perspectives 

In this work, the ensemble-variational (EnVar) framework is used for extracting steady flow control strategies in open-cavity configurations, dealing with high dimensional parameter space. This study ad-dresses a gap in the application of EnVar methods to high-dimensional optimisation problems by using a formulation in which the cost-function gradient is estimated using directional finite di ff erences within a fixed ensemble subspace. The sensitivity information resulting from this process is then projected onto the ensemble subspace and supplied to standard optimisation algorithms, such as Gauss–Newton–based meth-ods and L-BFGS-B. Compared to classical ensemble-based strategies, this method e ff ectively removes the need for heuristic decisions related to step-size selection or the regeneration of ensemble members at each it-eration. Since the ensemble matrix remains constant, optimization occurs within a consistent reduced-order 23 subspace, ensuring a stable and interpretable solution. The methodology is evaluated across two distinct flow regimes: a quasi-periodic regime at Re = 6250 and a chaotic regime at Re = 14000. The primary objective is to demonstrate the e ff ectiveness of non-intrusive, ensemble-based gradient estimation as a robust alternative to classical adjoint-based optimization. Results for the moderate Reynolds number regime ( Re = 6250) confirm that the EnVar approach identifies optimal steady forcing strategies highly consistent with those obtained via adjoint-based methods. Both methodologies achieve a significant reduction in kinetic energy fluctuations, e ff ectively stabilizing the flow and driving its dynamics from a complex quasi-periodic state toward a periodic limit cycle. Sensitivity analysis reveals that an ensemble size of approximately Nens ≈ 20 su ffi ces to capture the dominant sys-tem sensitivities, providing an accurate gradient approximation even in a high-dimensional control space. Application of the framework to the chaotic regime at Re = 14000 highlights the critical role of the optimi-sation algorithm within the EnVar setting. In this more nonlinear regime, the cost function is expected to be more di ffi cult to minimise, and the Gauss–Newton method exhibits a slower reduction of the objective. By contrast, the L-BFGS-B algorithm proves more robust, achieving rapid convergence and leading to a strong reduction of the Reynolds stresses, particularly near the cavity leading edge. From a computational perspective, the EnVar framework provides several notable advantages. Its non-intrusive formulation allows direct coupling with existing high-fidelity CFD solvers, avoiding the develop-ment and maintenance of dedicated adjoint implementations. In addition, the ensemble evaluations are nat-urally parallelizable, leading to substantial reductions in wall-clock time relative to the inherently sequential forward–adjoint approach. These features make EnVar particularly well suited for large-scale engineering applications where computational resources are available but adjoint solvers are di ffi cult to implement or su ff er from numerical sensitivity. Moreover, the method remains applicable in regimes where numerical instabilities compromise the reliability of adjoint gradient calculations. In conclusion, this study demonstrates that ensemble-variational methods provide a versatile and robust methodology for flow optimization in complex unsteady regimes. Future work will focus on extending this framework to three-dimensional configurations and investigating its performance in fully turbulent flows. Additionally, exploring time-dependent (harmonic) forcing strategies within the EnVar approach could fur-ther enhance the ability to control self-sustained oscillations and complex attractors across a broader range of fluid dynamics problems.

The posterior statistics were computed from the solutions obtained for Re = 6250 (figure 13) using di ff erent ensemble sizes. The uncertainty maps reported in figures B.21 and B.22 exhibit a dependence on the ensemble size. As the number of ensemble members increases from Nens = 5 to Nens = 40, the estimated uncertainty becomes progressively richer and more spatially extended. This behaviour is expected, since a larger ensemble provides a more complete representation of the admissible perturbation subspace, whereas a small ensemble can only span a limited number of modes and therefore tends to underestimate the actual uncertainty level. For low values of Nens , the uncertainty field remains artificially confined, reflecting the limited ability of a reduced ensemble to capture the full variability permitted by the prior model. As Nens increases, the uncertainty level rises in the regions that are less dynamically relevant to the optimisation and progressively approaches the prior distribution shown in figure 3. This indicates that, away from the most sensitive areas, the observations do not strongly constrain the control, so that the posterior uncertainty remains close to the background one. By contrast, the region near the cavity edge consistently exhibits lower uncertainty, even for the largest ensemble sizes. This suggests that this region plays a dominant role in the optimisation process: the control there is more e ff ectively constrained, and the solutions obtained with di ff erent ensemble sizes remain close to one another. The persistence of low uncertainty in this area reflects its dynamical importance, whereas the larger uncertainty observed elsewhere is associated with directions that are less influential on the objective functional.
