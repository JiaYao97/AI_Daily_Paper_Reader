# When can a neural operator replace a coarse solve? Architectural principles for two-level preconditioning 

## Hugo Melchers, Michael Abdelmalik, Victorita Dolean May 20, 2026 

Abstract 

Neural operators are increasingly used as drop-in accelerators inside classical numerical methods, but it is rarely clear which architectural ingredients matter for which role. We answer this question for one important role: the coarse-space correction inside a two-level pre-conditioner for discretised linear partial differential equations. By systematically varying four DeepONet-like architectures along two design axes — input discretisation (sampling versus integration against a basis) and source-term linearity — we show that the favourable corner of this 2 ×2 design is occupied by a single architecture, the Neural Green’s Operator (NGO), and that moving away from it produces predictable failure modes: structurally non-symmetric preconditioned spectra, breakdown of preconditioned conjugate gradients on self-adjoint prob-lems, and stagnation on non-self-adjoint ones. Used as a coarse-space correction, the NGO matches the iteration count of an exact coarse solve on diffusion and advection-diffusion prob-lems. We also characterise the failure of fixed-size learned coarse spaces at high Helmholtz wave numbers, isolating it as a property of the basis rather than of the architecture. The principle generalises: integrating inputs against the basis used for the output is what allows a neural operator to serve as a Galerkin-type coarse-space correction. 

# 1 Introduction 

Solving large, sparse linear systems arising from the discretisation of partial differential equations (PDEs) is a workhorse computation across the physical sciences. Iterative Krylov solvers — con-jugate gradients (CG) and the generalised minimal residual method (GMRES) and their variants — dominate this regime, but their convergence depends critically on the choice of preconditioner. Constructing preconditioners that are simultaneously robust (effective across a parameter range), 

cheap to apply (ideally with cost scaling linearly in the number of unknowns), and compatible with the Krylov method of choice (e.g. symmetric and positive definite for CG) remains a central chal-lenge of numerical linear algebra. Two-level preconditioners — which combine a local smoother 

with a global coarse-space correction — are an established answer to this challenge [13, 49, 53], with multigrid the canonical and often gold-standard instance [53]. Over the last decade, machine learning has emerged as a complementary route. Neural operators [29, 34, 38] approximate maps between function spaces and so, in principle, learn the solution operator of a parametric PDE directly. By themselves, however, neural operators rarely deliver the accuracy required by downstream applications, and they offer no guarantee that the predicted field satisfies the discrete equations. A natural way to harness their speed without sacrificing reliability is to use them as preconditioners inside a classical Krylov method: an accurate neural operator then accelerates an iterative solver whose final accuracy is governed only by the discretisation and a user-set tolerance, not by the network [45, 56]. This division of labour is the line of work this paper contributes to. 

Families of learned preconditioners Existing approaches to learning preconditioners can be grouped into three broad families. 1

• Algebraic preconditioners take the matrix A alone and produce, via a neural network, an approximation to A−1 or to a sparse factorisation thereof. Examples include direct inverse approximation [46], learned incomplete LU/Cholesky factorisations [19, 20, 31, 32], and graph-neural-network preconditioners [9, 52]. These methods are PDE-agnostic but typically do not exploit the structure of the underlying equation. 

• Hybrid iterative methods alternate a neural operator with a classical smoother inside a fixed-point iteration. The Hybrid Iterative Numerical Transferable Solver (HINTS) [56] is the most prominent representative, with closely related schemes proposed in [23, 27]. Because the neural component is non-linear, these methods are generally combined with flexible variants of the Krylov method, e.g. flexible GMRES (F-GMRES) [47] or flexible CG [45]. Recent analyses, however, show that HINTS-style methods can stagnate or diverge unless the training paradigm is chosen carefully [55]. 

• Multigrid- and deflation-based learned preconditioners use a learned component as a coarse-grid solver [1, 30, 36] or as a deflation/coarse subspace [28]. This is the family the present work belongs to. The methodology of Kopaniˇ c´ akov´ a et al. [28] is particularly close to ours: there, a DeepONet trunk net is trained explicitly so that its basis can be repurposed as a deflation subspace inside PCG. The methods above are increasingly competitive with classical preconditioners, but they typically introduce a learned component whose internal structure is opaque and whose effectiveness is doc-umented empirically rather than tied to specific architectural choices. 

What this paper proposes A two-level preconditioner has two components: the smoother and the coarse-space correction. The smoother is well understood and largely PDE-specific; the coarse-space correction, by contrast, is exactly what a neural operator naturally approximates — a global map from a right-hand side to an approximate solution, expressed as a linear combination of basis functions. This raises a sharp question: which architectural properties of a neural operator govern its effectiveness as a coarse-space correction? Most existing studies pick one architecture and tune it; very few isolate the architectural ingredients that matter. To answer this question, we keep the smoother and the coarse basis φ1, . . . , φ m fixed and vary only the neural operator that maps right-hand sides to coarse-space coefficients. We compare four DeepONet-like architectures — DeepONet [38], RINO [2], VarMiON [43], and the Neural Green’s Operator (NGO) [40] — chosen because they sit at the four corners of a 2 ×2 design matrix:

Contributions We make four contributions. 1. An architectural principle for learned coarse corrections. We show that the way a neural operator discretises its inputs — by sampling versus by integration against a basis — is the dominant architectural factor controlling its effectiveness as a coarse-space correction inside a two-level preconditioner. Sampling-based architectures (DeepONet, VarMiON) pro-duce learned operators whose row and column spaces differ structurally, leading to spectra with non-negligible imaginary parts even on self-adjoint problems and to PCG breakdown on diffusion. Integration-based architectures (RINO, NGO) remove this asymmetry. 22. The role of linearity. We show that whether a neural operator preserves the linear de-pendence of the PDE solution on the source term is a secondary but consequential design axis. Linearity becomes essential precisely when the spectrum of the discrete operator is structurally complex (advection-diffusion, Helmholtz), separating the NGO from the RINO. 3. NGO-based coarse corrections are competitive with exact coarse solves. On the diffusion and advection-diffusion equations, an NGO-based coarse correction matches an exact coarse solve in iteration count, while requiring no recursive linear solve at the coarse level. On the Helmholtz equation it remains the best of the four learned architectures but reveals a fundamental limitation of fixed-size coarse spaces at high wave numbers, suggesting a concrete direction for future work. 4. NGO-based coarse corrections can outperform algebraic multigrid. When solving diffusion equations with PCG, the two-level preconditioner with NGO-based coarse correction is computationally cheap to construct and to apply, resulting in an improvement in wall-time taken to obtain the solution when compared to algebraic multigrid with PyAMG. We also identify two methodological conditions on which all of the above hinges: (i) the training data set must populate the entire coarse space, not just a subset of it (Section 4); and (ii) basis functions supported on Dirichlet boundaries must be excluded from the coarse correction. Both lessons are easily missed and have, to our knowledge, not been articulated in this form in prior work. 

Scope This work primarily focuses on iteration counts , not wall-clock time. The neural operators we use are evaluated on relatively small 2D problems (up to ∼ 10 5 unknowns); a fair runtime com-parison against optimised classical preconditioners would be most useful for more computationally difficult problems and is left to follow-up work. However, we perform a preliminary performance comparison which shows that neural-operator-based coarse solvers can be more computationally ef-ficient than their exact counterparts and make two-level preconditioners competitive with algebraic multi-grid for diffusion problems. We also do not claim that an NGO is the “best” learned precon-ditioner for any of these PDEs in absolute terms; our claim is that, among neural-operator-based coarse-space corrections built from a fixed basis, the integrate-and-linear architecture systemati-cally outperforms its sample-based and non-linear counterparts, and matches an exact coarse solve on a few test problems. 

Outline Section 2 reviews two-level preconditioning and the four neural-operator architectures we test, emphasising the structural differences that turn out to matter. Section 3 describes how each architecture is adapted to act as a coarse-space correction on a finite-difference or finite-volume discretisation. Section 4 presents the numerical experiments on diffusion, advection-diffusion, and Helmholtz problems, including a spectral analysis that explains the observed differences. Section 5 draws conclusions and lists open problems. 

# 2 Preliminaries 

This section recalls the two ingredients combined in the rest of the paper. Section 2.1 fixes no-tation and reviews two-level preconditioning, and Section 2.2 reviews the four neural-operator architectures whose use as coarse-space corrections will be compared. 

## 2.1 Two-level preconditioning 

A two-level preconditioner combines a local correction — the smoother — with a global correction defined on a low-dimensional subspace — the coarse-space correction . The construction goes back to deflation [41], balancing domain-decomposition methods [39], and the multigrid coarse-grid 3correction [53]. A general two-level preconditioner has the following components; we follow the unified notation of Tang et al. [49]. 

• A large n × n linear system A u = b, arising from the discretisation of a PDE. 

• A matrix Z ∈ Rn×m with m < n , called the coarse basis ; its column space is the coarse space .

• The coarse matrix E = ZT A Z ∈ Rm×m.

• The coarse-space correction Q = Z E −1 ZT, which is the A-orthogonal projection of A−1

onto the coarse space. 

• A smoother M−1, typically derived from an incomplete factorisation (e.g. ILU or incomplete Cholesky) or from a domain-decomposition method such as additive Schwarz; see [51, Ch. 2] for a textbook treatment. The two components Q and M−1 must then be combined to form a single preconditioner. The simplest combination is the additive one, 

PAD = M−1 + Q, (1) in which the smoother and the coarse-space correction are simply summed. A richer family of combinations is obtained by applying one component to a residual from which the contribution of the other component has already been projected out — a deflated or hybrid combination. For the two-component case relevant here, this gives the two adapted-deflation forms 

PA-DEF1 = Q + M−1 (I − A Q ), (2a) 

PA-DEF2 = M−1 + Q (I − A M −1), (2b) which differ only in the order in which the two corrections are applied. A symmetric variant of these forms is the balancing-Neumann-Neumann preconditioner [39], 

PBNN = Q + ( I − Q A ) M−1 (I − A Q ), (3) which is symmetric whenever both Q and M−1 are. Equivalently, (2a)–(3) can be implemented as one or two steps of fixed-point iteration on the unpreconditioned residual: starting from w0 = 0,each component is applied in turn to the current residual v − A w j and the result is added to wj .We use this fixed-point form in our implementation. The four labels AD, A-DEF1, A-DEF2, and BNN are standard [49], and the four precondition-ers (1)–(3) are exactly those compared in our experiments. Note that in domain-decomposition methods the smoother M−1 may itself be a combination of subdomain solvers, opening up further hybrid combinations [51]. We take M−1 as fixed throughout and do not consider such nested hybrids here. While two-level preconditioning has been shown to be very effective, one difficulty is the matrix 

E−1 appearing in Q. Each application of Q requires solving a m × m linear system E w = v.This solve does not have to be exact as long as the resulting preconditioner is still effective; in multigrid methods, for example, E−1 is approximated recursively. The starting point of this paper is that an accurate neural operator can play the same role: it can stand in for an exact coarse solve, evaluated by a single forward pass and without a recursive linear solver. 

## 2.2 Neural operators 

Whereas standard machine-learning models learn maps between vector spaces, neural operators 

learn maps between function spaces [29]. They are a natural choice for approximating PDE solution operators, where the inputs and outputs — coefficients, source terms, boundary data, and the solution itself — are all functions on the PDE domain or its boundary. 4A wide variety of neural operator architectures has been developed since [38, 34], including Deep-ONets and their variants [2, 26, 27, 40, 43], Fourier Neural Operators (FNO) [34] and their extensions [5, 35], transformer-based operators [8, 17], convolutional neural operators [44], and resolution-invariant variants for complex geometries [24]. These architectures differ markedly in how they handle the input fields and how they represent the output function. This work focuses on the DeepONet-like family: architectures whose output is a linear combination of a finite set of basis functions. We restrict to this family for two reasons. First, this restriction enables a more direct comparison: every architecture we consider produces a function in the same finite-dimensional space, so differences in performance can be attributed to differences in the model rather than to differences in the output representation. Second, models with this property can be interpreted directly as coarse-space corrections inside a two-level preconditioner: the basis functions φ1, . . . , φ k, evaluated at the discretisation nodes, define the coarse basis Z, and the coefficients produced by the network play the role of the coarse-space coefficients (see Section 3). Within this family, the four architectures we compare differ along two axes: 1. how the input fields are discretised — by sampling at sensor nodes, or by integration against a function basis; 2. whether the architecture preserves the linear dependence of the PDE solution on the source-term inputs. The four resulting combinations are realised by DeepONet, RINO, VarMiON, and NGO respec-tively, as summarised in Table 1. DeepONet Introduced by Lu et al. [38] and based on the universal-approximation result of Chen and Chen [10], the Deep Operator Network (DeepONet) is a widely used neural operator architecture. Each input field is sampled at a fixed set of sensor nodes , and the resulting fixed-size vectors are concatenated and passed to a deep network — the branch net — that outputs coefficients in a basis represented by a second deep network — the trunk net . In Table 1, the DeepONet sits in the top-right cell: it samples its inputs (top row) and depends non-linearly on all input fields, including the source term (right column). 

RINO Bahmani et al. [2] introduced two related architectures, the Resolution-Invariant Deep-ONet (RI-DeepONet) and the Resolution-Invariant Neural Operator (RINO). Both replace the sampling step of the DeepONet by projection of the input fields onto a function basis: the inputs to the branch net are basis coefficients rather than pointwise values. This makes the architec-ture “resolution-invariant”, in the sense that the input fields can be specified on any set of points equipped with quadrature weights, rather than on a fixed sensor grid. The RINO additionally fixes the basis (rather than learning a trunk) and trains the model to predict basis coefficients of the solution; the RI-DeepONet keeps a learnable trunk and trains for solution accuracy. To enable a like-for-like comparison with the other architectures, we use a hybrid that we will simply refer to as the RINO throughout this paper: the basis is fixed (as in the original RINO) but the model is trained to minimise the error in the solution function (as in the RI-DeepONet). [43] introduce the Variationally Mimetic Operator Network (VarMiON), which is aimed specifically at linear PDEs. The key architectural choice is to handle the PDE coefficients separately from the source terms: the coefficients enter a non-linear branch network that produces a parameter-dependent matrix, and the source-term inputs (sampled at sensor nodes) enter linearly through a matrix-vector multiplication with this matrix. The resulting model is non-linear in the PDE coefficients but linear in the source terms , mimicking the structure of the underlying linear PDE solution operator. The VarMiON is one of several DeepONet variants that separate the dependence on different input fields; another is the Multiple-Input Operator Network (MIONet) [26], with closely related modifications appearing in [27, 28]. We focus on the VarMiON because it most closely matches the linear structure of the PDE problems considered here. For other (non-linear) PDEs other MIONet-like architectures may be more suitable. In Table 1, the VarMiON sits in the top-left cell: it samples its inputs (top row) and is linear in the source terms (left column). 

Neural Green’s Operator The Neural Green’s Operator (NGO) is described in Melchers et al. [40]. For our purposes, the NGO can be thought of as combining the architectural fea-tures of the RINO and the VarMiON. First, it does not sample its input fields but discretises them by integration against the basis functions. Second, it preserves the linear dependence of the output on the source terms while learning the non-linear dependence on the PDE coefficients. In Table 1, the NGO therefore sits in the bottom-left cell. This combination is not arbitrary. The exact solution operator of a linear PDE is, by definition, the integral against a Green’s function, and is linear in the source terms. The NGO is the only one of the four architectures we consider that mimics both of these structural properties. The central empirical question of this paper is whether mimicking these structural properties also makes the NGO a better coarse-space correction — and, if so, which of the two properties is responsible. 

# 3 Neural operators as coarse-space corrections 

A two-level coarse-space correction is determined by two ingredients that play very different roles. The first is the coarse basis Z ∈ Rn×m, which fixes the subspace span( Z) into which residuals are projected and within which the correction is sought; the choice of Z is a structural decision about 

which part of A−1 the correction is meant to capture. The second is the coarse operator 

E = ZT A Z ∈ Rm×m, (4) which is a Galerkin restriction of A to the coarse space and which determines how a residual is mapped to its coarse-space best approximant. Together they yield the canonical coarse-space correction Q = Z E −1 ZT, which can be read as a three-stage pipeline:

It is therefore natural to ask whether the first part of Q, i.e. E−1ZT — and only this part — can be approximated by a learned surrogate that maps coarse-space residuals to coarse-space coefficients, while leaving Z untouched. This separation of concerns is the starting point of the present paper, and it is what distinguishes the architectural question we ask from the basis-design question asked in some recent neighbouring work. In Kopaniˇ c´ akov´ a et al. [28], for instance, the basis itself is learned (a DeepONet trunk net is trained so that its output basis functions form an effective deflation subspace), so Z is the object being adapted and the action of E−1 on the coarse space is performed exactly. We do the opposite: Z is fixed at the start of the experiments using a problem-agnostic basis (a uniform B-spline grid), and what varies between methods is only the surrogate for E−1ZT. Each of the four neural operators considered in this work expresses its output as a linear combi-nation of fixed basis functions φ1, . . . , φ m, evaluated on the discretisation nodes. What the four architectures of Table 1 differ in is precisely how they implement this composite operator — in particular, whether the projection onto the coarse space is realised by sampling or by integration against the basis, and whether the dependence on the right-hand side is linear or non-linear. We make these implementations explicit in Section 3.2. This substitution is attractive for three reasons. First, it inherits the standard guarantee of preconditioning-based methods: the accuracy of the final solution depends only on the discreti-sation and the Krylov tolerance, not on the accuracy of the neural operator, which only affects convergence rate. Second, evaluating a neural operator does not require a recursive linear solve at the coarse level — a single forward pass replaces the iterative or direct inversion of E.

Remark 3.1 (Scope of the architectural axes) . The two design axes of Table 1 apply to the surrogate for E−1ZT, not to the basis Z itself. In particular, when the basis Z is itself the wrong object — for instance, if its dimension is too small to capture the relevant near-kernel of A,as happens for high-wave number Helmholtz (Section 4.3) — then no choice of architecture for the surrogate can recover what is missing from the basis. The architectural question we study is therefore well-posed only on top of an adequate basis.

# 4 Numerical experiments 

This section tests the predictions of Section 3 on three prototypical PDEs of increasing difficulty: diffusion (self-adjoint and positive definite), advection-diffusion (non-self-adjoint), and Helmholtz (indefinite). Section 3 predicts that the integration-vs-sampling axis of Table 1 should govern whether a neural-operator-based preconditioner can be made symmetric, and that the linearity axis should govern its behaviour on non-self-adjoint problems. The three test problems isolate these predictions in turn. The general protocol is the same in all three cases. We generate a training data set by sampling and solving a large number of realisations of the PDE; train each neural operator on 80% of the data set, using another 10% as validation data to prevent overfitting; and finally discretise the remaining 10% of the realisations and solve the resulting linear systems with various Krylov methods, comparing different choices of coarse-space correction. The full PDE definitions are in Appendix A, the smoothers in Appendix B, and the network architectures and training procedure in Appendix C. 

Baselines and benchmarks The natural classical benchmark for these problems is multigrid [53], which is the gold-standard preconditioner for diffusion and remains effective for advection-diffusion in many regimes. For Helmholtz, multigrid alone is insufficient; the state-of-the-art classical preconditioners combine a shifted-Laplacian or domain decomposition smoother with a wave-number-dependent coarse space such as DtN [11], GenEO [7], or ∆ k-GenEO [12]. Because the present work focuses on architectural principles for the learned coarse correction rather than on optimal classical methods, we use as our reference benchmark the exact two-level preconditioner — i.e., the same combination structure with a direct solve of the coarse system E w = v — alongside two further baselines: the smoother alone (no coarse correction), and no preconditioning at all. Our implementation is unoptimised Python on relatively small 2D problems, so timings would not be representative of what an optimised implementation can achieve on either the classical or the learned side of the comparison. Iteration counts are mesh-independent up to the standard second-order tolerance scaling, and they cleanly separate algorithmic effects from implementation effects. However, we compare the wall-clock time for the diffusion problem in Section 4.4, which shows that two-level preconditioners can significantly speed up two-level preconditioners and result in performance that is competitive with classical methods. Section 4.5 returns to this point.

## 4.1 Diffusion equation 

The diffusion equation is the simplest of our three test problems: self-adjoint, positive definite, and amenable to multigrid [53]. We use it for four purposes. First, as a baseline to check that learned coarse-space corrections can in principle accelerate Krylov methods. Second, to study the effect of the training data distribution on the resulting preconditioner. Third, to expose an interaction between the coarse space and the discretisation of Dirichlet boundary conditions that turns out to matter substantially. Fourth, since diffusion is normally solved with PCG, to test whether the predictions of Section 3.2 on the symmetry of the learned preconditioner (and hence its compatibility with PCG) hold up empirically.

The mismatch between the mesh-dependent coarse-matrix spectrum and the mesh-independent learned operator destroys the preconditioner’s effectiveness as the mesh is refined. Removing the boundary-supported basis functions removes the unstable directions from the coarse space, restoring effectiveness. We use this “trimmed” coarse space throughout the remainder of the paper. This boundary-handling condition is, to our knowledge, not articulated in prior work, and it has the convenient feature of being trivially enforceable: it does not require retraining the model, only zeroing out a subset of its output coefficients.

## 4.2 Advection-diffusion equation: the linearity axis becomes decisive 

The advection-diffusion equation is non-self-adjoint: its discrete operator A is no longer symmetric, even for symmetric domains and constant coefficients. Section 3.2 predicts that on such problems the second axis of Table 1 — whether the architecture preserves the linear dependence of the PDE solution on the source term — becomes decisive. The intuition is that an architecture that is non-linear in the source can model the asymmetry of A only through its dependence on the source, which leads to spectra that are spread rather than concentrated. The advection-diffusion experiments test this directly. We use the same B-spline source distribution as Dataset 3 of the diffusion experiments (the only one effective for training). To test out-of-distribution generalisation we additionally construct a high-P´ eclet test set with substantially larger advective velocities than appear in training. The PDE definitions, the four-direction Gauss–Seidel smoother, and the data-set construction are detailed in Appendix A.2. The headline results are in Figure 9. Three behaviours are visible. First, the four-direction Gauss– Seidel smoother is already a strong preconditioner on its own, so the bar to clear is high. Second, the NGO-based correction matches the exact coarse solve across all five mesh resolutions, both in- and out-of-distribution. Third — and this is the new behaviour relative to diffusion — only the NGO produces a consistent speed-up over the smoother. The RINO is competitive at the finest meshes but counterproductive at the coarsest; the VarMiON helps only at the coarsest mesh and is harmful elsewhere; the DeepONet typically fails to converge altogether. The advection-diffusion problem thus separates the four architectures along both design axes simultaneously. The integration-vs-sampling axis still distinguishes the convergent (RINO, NGO) from the often-failing (DeepONet, VarMiON), as before; but on top of this, the linearity axis now picks out the NGO as the only consistently effective choice. ## 4.3 Helmholtz equation: a fixed-size coarse space is not enough 

The Helmholtz equation is qualitatively harder than the previous two cases. It is indefinite: −∆−k2

has both positive and negative eigenvalues, with the negative ones corresponding to oscillatory modes whose number scales with kd in dimension d. The discrete operator A inherits this indefi-niteness, and the resulting linear systems are notoriously hard to solve. 

Classical challenges The classical literature on Helmholtz coarse spaces is unanimous on one point: an effective coarse space for indefinite Helmholtz cannot be wave-number-independent . Stan-dard analyses of two-level domain-decomposition methods for Helmholtz [4, 7, 11, 12, 15] all show, by analysis or by numerical experiment, that the dimension of an effective coarse space must grow with k. The empirical comparisons of Bootland et al. [6] make the consequences concrete: only spectral coarse spaces such as DtN [11] or GenEO — whose dimensions are automatically chosen to track k — yield wave-number-robust convergence. Resolving the latter is a prerequisite for even attempting the former.

The numerical experiments above are deliberately scoped to isolate the architectural axes of Table 1: same coarse basis, same smoother, same training pipeline, same metrics. This isolation comes at the cost of two limitations that are worth stating clearly, together with the directions in which an extended study could go. 

2D only All test problems are posed on the unit square. A full 3D study would test whether the architectural ranking established in 2D persists in higher dimension, whether the integration-based architectures retain their resolution-invariance benefit on tensorised 3D quadrature rules, and whether the high-frequency basis-coverage condition identified in Section 4.1 (Dataset 3) carries the same weight when the coarse-space dimension grows from k = 144 to k = O(10 3). The most informative single 3D extension would be a 3D variant of the diffusion problem on a structured grid with ∼10 6 unknowns. The NGO architecture is unchanged in 3D — the basis functions and quadrature weights are simply tensor-products of their 2D counterparts — so retraining and testing in 3D would be a direct extension rather than a re-design. We have not pursued this here because the goal of the present paper is to establish the architectural principles cleanly; an extended 3D study is the natural sequel. 

Exact coarse solves as reference We benchmark all four learned methods against the exact two-level preconditioner (with the same basis and a direct solve of E w = v) and against the smoother alone. This is the right benchmark for the question we ask — can the learned surrogate match what an exact coarse solve does? — but it is not the same as benchmarking against the strongest classical preconditioner available for each PDE. Two concrete extensions would tighten this story. 

• For all three PDEs: comparison against incomplete factorisations (ILU, IC) used as precon-ditioners on their own, to provide a PDE-agnostic baseline that does not require choosing a smoother. 

• For Helmholtz: comparison against shifted-Laplacian multigrid or a classical wave-number-dependent coarse space such as DtN or Hk-GenEO. As argued in Section 4.3, no fixed-basis learned method can be expected to match these at high k; the comparison would quantify the gap. None of these extensions is technically difficult; we omit them because they would not affect the architectural conclusions of the paper. 

# 5 Conclusion 

We have studied a clean architectural question: when a neural operator is used as a coarse-space correction inside a two-level preconditioner with a fixed coarse basis, which of its design choices matter? The four-corner design grid of Table 1 isolated two axes — whether the input fields are discretised by sampling or by integration against the basis, and whether the network preserves the linear dependence of the solution on the source term. The numerical experiments confirmed the prediction made in Section 3.2: integration is the dominant axis, controlling whether the resulting preconditioner can be made symmetric and therefore whether it is even compatible with PCG; linearity is the secondary but consequential axis, determining whether the preconditioned spectrum stays well-clustered on non-self-adjoint problems. Among the four architectures tested, only the Neural Green’s Operator (NGO) sits at the favourable corner of both axes, and only the NGO matches an exact coarse solve in iteration count on diffusion and advection-diffusion. 24 Practical recommendations Two design choices are recommended for any neural-operator-based coarse-space correction. First, the source-term inputs should be integrated against the coarse basis rather than sampled at fixed sensor nodes; this aligns the row and column spaces of the learned operator and is what allows it to be symmetrised when the underlying problem is self-adjoint. Second, when the PDE is linear, the architecture should preserve the linear dependence of the solution on the source; this rules out spurious input-dependent variation in the spectrum of the preconditioned matrix. Two methodological conditions are also necessary: the training data must populate the entire coarse space (not just a low-dimensional subset of it), and basis functions supported on Dirichlet boundaries must be excluded from the coarse correction. The latter is a small price: it can be enforced trivially by zeroing out the corresponding coefficients in the network output, requires no retraining, and — as we found in practice for the RINO — is not associated with any loss of accuracy on the interior of the domain. 

Helmholtz The Helmholtz experiments make precise where the present approach breaks down: at high wave numbers, all four architectures deteriorate and lose to the exact coarse solve. Sec-tion 4.3 traced this back to the fixed dimension of the coarse basis. Classical Helmholtz coarse-space theory [6, 7, 12] shows that any robust coarse space must have dimension scaling with k, and a fixed B-spline basis cannot satisfy this regardless of the network used to map residuals to coefficients. The natural way forward is therefore not to redesign the architecture but to redesign the basis: replace the fixed B-spline coarse space with a wave-number-aware one, either by enrichment with plane-wave modes or by combining the learned coarse correction with a classical wave-number-dependent coarse space. This bridge between learned coarse corrections and classical Helmholtz coarse-space theory is the most concrete open problem identified by the present work. 

Other directions Beyond the extensions discussed in Section 4.5, four further directions stand out. First, application to PDEs whose coarse-space behaviour is qualitatively different — saddle-point systems arising from Stokes and Oseen problems are a natural target. Second, alternatives to data-driven training: manufactured solutions [18] or physics-informed losses [33] would remove the need to solve many PDE realisations to generate training data. Third, training objectives aimed specifically at producing good preconditioners; the present results show that an L2 loss on the solution already produces useful preconditioners, but a loss that targets the spectral properties relevant to Krylov convergence [40] could plausibly close the residual gap to the exact coarse solve. Finally, the use of more computationally efficient architectures is necessary for learned coarse corrections to be competitive with classical preconditioners in terms of wall-clock time. Code and trained models reproducing all experiments will be made available upon acceptance of the manuscript.

To help the DeepONet and RINO, i.e. the non-linear models, to generalise to right-hand-side vectors outside of the training data distribution, these models scale their inputs so as to ensure positive homogeneity in the source terms. This symmetry is embedded into each model by passing all four mirrored versions of each PDE problem to the model, ‘un-mirroring’ the resulting predictions, and averaging.
