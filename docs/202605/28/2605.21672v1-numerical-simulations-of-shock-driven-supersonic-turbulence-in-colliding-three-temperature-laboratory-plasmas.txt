Submitted to ApJ ABSTRACT Shock-driven turbulence is central to astrophysical plasmas in which explosions and compressive driving inject energy through shocks rather than steady stirring. We present three-dimensional, three-temperature (ion, electron, and radiation; 3T) radiation-hydrodynamic simulations of a laboratory platform in which two offset CH mesh targets are irradiated by a 30 ns X-ray pulse. Mesh ablation launches counter-streaming supersonic flows whose vorticity is seeded baroclinically at mesh-cell cor-ners, advected into collimated channels over ∼ 15 ns, and injected into the outgoing streams before collision. The flows first collide at t ≃ 75 ns, forming a shocked turbulent mixing layer that persists for at least 300 ns, reaches ℓ0 ≃ 4.5 mm, and evolves toward an effectively isothermal equation of state with γeff ≃ 1.1. After stagnation, u0(t) ∝ t−1.1 while t0/t cs ≃ 0.2 remains nearly fixed. Compression and stretching dominate the vorticity budget, and the velocity field relaxes toward a kinetic-energy partition of approximately 70% solenoidal and 30% compressive. The Reynolds stress is strongly anisotropic at the outer scale and remains measurably anisotropic over much of the resolved inertial interval, indicating directional memory of the collision axis and mesh geometry across many scales. The solenoidal strain spectrum implies ℓν, s ≃ 92 μm, ℓ0/ℓ ν, s ≃ 49, and an effective Reynolds number Re ∼ 2 × 10 2. The density-gradient spectrum is directly tied to the compressive mode spectrum, which evolves independently from the incompressible cascade. These results establish the double-mesh platform as a controlled laboratory realisation of sustained shock-driven turbulence and a quantitative baseline for future high-energy-density laboratory astrophysics experiments and diagnostics. 

Keywords: HEDP, Laboratory Astrophysics, turbulence

The balance between compres-sive and solenoidal driving is itself an observable and model parameter of growing interest in both astrophys-ical and laboratory shock-turbulence studies (Krumholz & Burkhart 2016; Sharda et al. 2022; Gerrard et al. 2023; Dhawalikar et al. 2022). Shock interaction with inhomo-geneous media can also drive post-shock turbulence and magnetic amplification (Hu et al. 2022; Hew & Federrath 2023). Such driving naturally produces shocks, strong density structure, and vorticity through baroclinic mis-alignment (Beattie et al. 2025; Beattie 2025), yielding turbulence with both |∇ × u|̸ = 0 and ∇ · u̸ = 0, where 

u is the fluid velocity, rather than purely incompress-ible turbulence with ∇ · u = 0 (Federrath et al. 2010; Federrath 2013; Beattie et al. 2025). Understanding how this kind of shock-driven turbulence is generated, evolves, and transfers energy across scale is therefore a central problem in both astrophysics and laboratory high-energy-density plasma physics. Directly diagnosing the internal structure of astro-physical shock-driven turbulence remains difficult be-cause radiative emission, projection, and line-of-sight in-tegration obscure the underlying flow (Gaensler et al. 2011). High-energy-density (HED) laboratory astro-physics experimental analogues offer a complementary route by generating shock-driven plasmas under con-trolled, diagnosable conditions. Previous HED plat-forms have established important aspects of blast-wave interaction and turbulence generation, including laser-driven blast waves (Tzeferacos et al. 2018a; Casner et al. 2018; Collins et al. 2020; Tzeferacos et al. 2018b; Bott et al. 2021; Bott et al. 2022) and shock tubes filled with low-density foams (Davidovits et al. 2022; Dhawalikar et al. 2022). What remains comparatively unexplored is a platform that produces a sustained, shock-rich turbu-lent mixing layer whose mode composition, large-scale anisotropy, and multiscale structure can be quantified. Before interpreting what future diagnostics may mea-sure in such systems, one must first establish the un-derlying turbulent state itself: how the turbulence is seeded, how compressive and solenoidal motions coex-ist, whether the large scales relax toward isotropy or re-tain memory of the driving geometry, how rapidly that directional memory weakens with decreasing scale, and what range of scales is dynamically resolved. Here we study such a platform: two offset, co-aligned CH mesh targets irradiated by a short-duration X-ray pulse, extending the class of pulsed-power-driven ra-diative plasma platforms discussed by Halliday et al. (2022). Figure 1 provides an overview of the plasma dy-namics in the system. The ablation launches counter-streaming supersonic flows whose structure is set by the mesh geometry already during the drive phase. Vortic-ity is seeded baroclinically near the mesh-cell corners, advected into collimated channels, and injected into the outgoing streams before the two flows collide. Their in-teraction forms a shocked, close-to-isothermal turbulent mixing layer that persists for several hundred nanosec-onds (longer than an outer-scale eddy turnover time) and supports a resolved mixed compressive–solenoidal cascade. This makes the platform well suited to estab-lishing the physics of shock-driven turbulence itself and to providing the baseline needed to interpret future lab-oratory diagnostics of such flows. In this paper, we use three-dimensional, three-temperature (3T) radiation-hydrodynamic simulations with flash to quantify the dynamics of this platform and the structure of the resulting turbulence. We first describe the numerical model and target configuration (Section 2), then follow the formation of the mixing layer, its integral measures, and the evolution of its outer-scale anisotropy (Section 3). We next show that the interaction region evolves toward an effectively close-to-isothermal thermodynamic closure (Section 4) and that its vorticity is seeded baroclinically at the mesh, ad-vected into collimated channels, and subsequently am-plified by compression and stretching after collision (Sec-tion 5). We then characterise the turbulence through Fourier spectra, Helmholtz mode decomposition, scale-dependent Reynolds-stress anisotropy, and an effective Reynolds-number estimate based on the resolved strain spectrum, thereby distinguishing persistent outer-scale directional memory from the much weaker anisotropy across most of the resolved cascade (Section 6). Fi-nally, we examine the density and density-gradient spec-tra, showing through the hierarchy in Equation 54 that scalar structure in the layer remains tied to compres-sive dynamics rather than the solenoidal cascade (Sec-tion 7). Taken together, these results provide a physical interpretation of how shock-driven turbulence is gener-ated in this platform and a quantitative baseline for the design and interpretation of future HED experiments.

A practical challenge in solving the coupled system of Equation 1, Equation 2, and Equation 3 is that the component-energy equations contain source terms pro-portional to ∇ · u in Equation 5, Equation 4, and Equa-tion 6. Across shock discontinuities, ∇ · u is undefined, so these terms cannot be evaluated directly. flash im-plements two standard strategies to handle this diffi-culty: the entropy-advection approach and the so-called RAGE-like approach, named after its implementation in the radiation-hydrodynamics code RAGE (Gittings et al. 2008). In the simulations presented here, we adopt the RAGE-like approach implemented in flash . In this method, shock heating is not assigned exclusively to Ei;instead, it is partitioned among Ei, Ee, and Erad in proportion to their partial pressures. This treatment is formally consistent with the smooth-flow equations, but it is not strictly correct at a shock, where the dissipative heating should enter only the ion energy equation. 2.2. Initial conditions and geometry of the setup 

The flash simulations are initialised to replicate a target design intended as a laboratory platform for studying plasma turbulence driven by soft-X-ray irra-diation from pulsed-power sources (Lebedev et al. 2001; Bland et al. 2006; Halliday et al. 2022; Merlini 2023; Marrow et al. 2026). The target system consists of two co-aligned CH meshes normal to the x direction and facing the incident X-ray source, as illustrated in Figure 3. The front mesh has high transparency (82% open area) with 2 × 2 mm square apertures and is lo-cated 1 mm from the emitting yz surface. The rear mesh has lower transparency (62% open area) with 2 × 1 mm end-slot features and is placed 9 mm behind the front mesh. Both meshes are initialised as plastic (CH) with density ρ = 1.05 g cm −3 at room temperature, with 

T = Ti = Te = Trad = 0 .025 eV (290 K). Although we employ CH tabulated EoS and opacity tables, we treat the ablated plasma as an effective single-species fluid, taking carbon as the representative constituent. To reduce computational cost while maintaining high resolution, we simulate only a subsection of the full tar-get system and use periodic transverse boundaries to emulate its lateral repetition. The surrounding vacuum regions are initialised with the same material proper-ties as the meshes but at a much lower density, ρvac =10 −8 g cm −3, and with temperature Tvac = 0.025 eV (290 K). Discretisation and numerical diffusion 

The computational domain is resolved with a uni-form 3D Cartesian grid of 1 ,200 × 750 × 500 cells, cov-ering a physical volume of 1 .2 cm × 0.75 cm × 0.5 cm. This corresponds to a uniform cell size of ∆ x = ∆ y =∆z = ∆ = 10 μm in all directions (see Figure 3). At this resolution we capture the large-scale flow structure together with a resolved solenoidal scale separation of about ℓ0/ℓ ν, s ∼ 50, even though the grid does not reach the true laboratory dissipation scale (see Section 6.3). No explicit sub-grid turbulence model, such as RANS or BHR, is evolved (e.g., Grete et al. 2015; Semenov 2025). Instead, the simulation operates in the implicit large-eddy simulation (ILES) regime, in which the nu-merical discretisation acts as an effective dissipation op-erator. In this sense, the resolved turbulent dynam-ics can be interpreted as a direct numerical simulation over the dynamically accessible range, with dissipation supplied by the numerical discretisation rather than by an explicit microphysical viscosity (Grete et al. 2023; Teissier et al. 2024; Shivakumar & Federrath 2025; Gre-han et al. 2025). As detailed in Section 6.3, we estimate the local dissi-pation (strain) scale and the effective Reynolds number by analysing the rate-of-strain tensor. For our third-order runs, this yields a solenoidal strain scale of ℓν, s ≃

92 μm ≃ 9∆, a resolved scale separation ℓ0/ℓ ν, s ≃ 49, and hence an effective numerical Reynolds number of or-der Re ∼ 2×10 2 under standard Kolmogorov scaling. To verify that the resolved scales adequately capture the es-sential turbulent statistics, we perform a reconstruction-order study (see Appendix A). TIME EVOLUTION OF THE SYSTEM AND INTEGRAL QUANTITIES 3.1. Global evolution of the mixing layer 

We first summarise the large-scale evolution of the system, following it from the ablation-driven launch of the mesh outflows to the emergence and growth of the central mixing layer, as illustrated in Figure 1. Figure 4 shows the evolution from the early target-ablation phase to the formation of the turbulent mixing layer. The plasma is driven by a 30-ns duration X-ray pulse, which ionizes the surface of the mesh targets. As ablation occurs, and as a result of strong pressure gra-dients, the superficial plasma is accelerated outwards, forming two counter-streaming supersonic outflows that propagate toward the mid-plane at x ≃ 6 mm. Each out-flow results from the collective merging of many adja-cent aperture-scale streams as the individual mesh holes fill with ablated material during the drive. Vorticity, 

ω = ∇ × u, seeded by the mesh geometry, is advected with these outflows and inherited by the central mixing layer; its origin is discussed in detail in Section 5. The outflows propagate toward the mid-plane with a characteristic bulk speed of order 40 km s −1, while their volume-averaged plasma properties within the in-flow control volume are summarised in Table 2. These averages give a characteristic mass density ⟨ρ⟩V ≃ 5 ×

10 −6 g cm −3, temperatures Ti ≃ Te ≃ 4 eV, and an ion-acoustic sound speed ⟨cs⟩V ≃ 9 km s −1. The near equality of Ti and Te is consistent with rapid electron– ion equilibration at these densities: τeq ≃ 2 ns, much shorter than the relevant dynamical timescales in the flow ( trad ∼ 30 ns, t0 ∼ 10 2 ns, and tcs ∼ 700 ns). This ordering justifies treating the inflows as close to thermal equilibrium when interpreting their bulk properties. Combining the bulk propagation speed with the inflow sound speed implies a bulk sonic Mach number M ≈ 4.5. This should be distinguished from the turbulent Mach number based on u0/c s discussed below. Amplification and transition in the mixing layer 

Once the seeded vortical inflows collide, the flow is no longer irrotational and the kinematic terms, e.g., 

−ω (∇ · u) and ω · ∇ ⊗ u in Equation 19 need not van-ish. The problem therefore shifts from vorticity genera-tion to vorticity amplification. The relevant question is which terms dominate the subsequent evolution of the inherited rotational content within the shocked interac-tion layer. In our platform, the collision acts primarily as an amplifier and re-organiser of pre-existing vortic-ity, rather than as the site of its initial creation. The resulting mixing layer remains mildly supersonic in the turbulent sense, with M ≃ 1.6 in the interaction control volume (see Table 2). This behaviour differs from the “self-generation” pic-ture of an unstable double-shock reverse system, in which vorticity is created primarily after the interaction of the counter-streaming flows (Markwick et al. 2021). It also refines the interpretation of the TDYNO laser-driven turbulent-dynamo platform, where offset grids are used to corrugate the ablation fronts and the sub-sequent collision of interleaving fronts is argued to gen-erate shear, Kelvin–Helmholtz instabilities, and turbu-lent mixing (Tzeferacos et al. 2017, 2018a). Our re-sults suggest that this collision-stage interpretation is incomplete: in any gridded flow with strong pressure and density gradients around aperture corners (as is the case for both radiation-driven and flow-past-grid ex-periments), the same baroclinic mechanism should seed rotational structure before the counter-streaming flows collide. The later Kelvin–Helmholtz-like shear and tur-bulent mixing would then amplify and reorganise an al-ready vortical upstream flow rather than generate tur-bulence from an initially laminar one. In this respect, the dynamics in our simulations are more closely analo-gous to shock-bounded interaction zones driven by non-uniform supersonic upstream structure, where down-stream turbulence develops through the combined action of inherited vorticity, shear, compression, and shock cur-vature (Folini & Walder 2006). Indeed, baroclinic gen-eration of vorticity is the key driver of supernova-driven turbulence (Beattie et al. 2025; Beattie 2025). The previous sections showed that the collision produces a shock-rich turbulent region whose vorticity is seeded at the mesh and subsequently amplified by compression and stretching. 8. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS We have presented three-dimensional, three-temperature radiation-hydrodynamic simulations of a double-mesh laboratory platform for generating shock-driven turbulence (Section 2, Figure 3, Figure 1). A

trad = 30 ns X-ray pulse incident on two CH meshes separated by 9 mm launches counter-streaming ablation flows from apertures of size 2 × 2 mm and 2 × 1 mm. These flows propagate toward the mid-plane at a bulk speed of order 40 km s −1, first collide at t ≃ 75 ns near 

x ≃ 6 mm, and then form a shock-rich mixing layer that remains active to at least t = 302 ns ≃ 1.15 t 0,nearly an order of magnitude longer than the drive itself (Section 3, Figure 4, Figure 5). The resulting chronology is quantitatively clear. Be-fore collision, the inflows have characteristic conditions 

ρ ≃ 5 × 10 −6 g cm −3, Ti ≃ Te ≃ 4 eV, and cs ≃ 9 km s −1,implying a bulk Mach number M ≈ 4.5 and an electron-ion equilibration time of only τeq ≃ 2 ns (Section 3, Ta-ble 2). At first contact the outer scale reaches ℓ0 ≃

4.5 mm inside a control volume of scale L = 0 .7 cm, while the largest eddy-turnover time is t0 = 261 ns and the ratio t0/t cs ≃ 0.2 remains approximately constant during the subsequent decay. Over that same post-stagnation phase, the turbulent velocity amplitude de-cays approximately as u0(t) ∝ t−1.1, consistent with freely decaying supersonic turbulence rather than sus-tained stationary forcing (Section 3, Figure 5, Equa-tion 16). The collision produces peak stagnation den-sities of order 10 −4 g cm −3 and heats the plasma from (Ti, T e) ≃ (4 , 4) eV to (24 , 10) eV, but without forming a simple planar post-shock slab (Section 3, Figure 7). The anisotropy analysis shows that the interaction retains directional memory, but mainly on the largest scales. The volume-integrated Reynolds stress relaxes only partially toward isotropy: Rxx /tr( R) decreases from near unity at stagnation to ≃ 0.72 by t/t 0 = 1 .15, while Ryy /tr( R) ≃ 0.16 and Rzz /tr( R) ≃ 0.12 remain unequal, demonstrating persistent memory of both the collision axis and the mesh geometry. This large-scale anisotropy is naturally tied to the pre-collision injection of narrow vortical jets by the mesh apertures discussed in Section 5. The scale-dependent Reynolds-stress anal-ysis then shows where that memory resides: the lowest modes remain close to a filament-like, streamwise state, but over 20 ≲ kL/ 2π ≲ 70 the component-wise spectral power is much closer to isotropic, with only the highest-

k tail turning away again after the onset of numerical dissipation (Section 3.3, Figure 6, Figure 14). This scale dependence is important for proton-radiography and proton-tomography interpretations, because commonly used stochastic-field inversions infer magnetic spectra and Brms under homogeneity and isotropy assumptions (Bott et al. 2017; Tzeferacos et al. 2018a; Bott et al. 2021). Our velocity anisotropy does not directly mea-sure magnetic anisotropy, but it shows that isotropy must be demonstrated, not assumed, on the injection scales of gridded flow platforms. The interaction layer also relaxes toward an effec-tively close-to-isothermal closure. Between t = 131 ns and 261 ns, the pressure-density relation evolves from 

γeff ≃ 1.16 to γeff ≃ 1.07 for the total pressure, while the radiation pressure remains nearly density-independent, 

γeff ≪ 1 (Section 4, Figure 7). This near-barotropic 24 response explains why sustained in-layer baroclinic gen-eration is weak even though the flow remains shock-rich. The key mechanism that generates vorticity, and therefore the incompressible component of the turbu-lence, occurs earlier, during the 30 ns ablation and hole-closure phase of each mesh cell. Oblique shocks inside the apertures create strong local misalignment between 

∇Ptot and ∇ρ, so the baroclinic source in Equation 19 generates predominantly ωx near the cell corners and along the off-bisector ribbons identified in Equation 20 and Equation 24. The converging in-plane flow then ad-vects this corner-generated vorticity toward the cell cen-tre and compresses it into narrow, geometry-dependent channels on a timescale ∆ tcoll ∼ 15 ns, implying in-plane advection speeds of order u⊥ ∼ 30–70 km s −1

for the front and rear cell geometries. The outflows therefore emerge already carrying collimated vortical jets rather than featureless compressive streams (Sec-tion 5, Figure 8). This mechanism should also operate in TDYNO-like gridded plasma flows, for which turbu-lence has been interpreted primarily as the product of shear and Kelvin–Helmholtz mixing when corrugated fronts interleave and collide (Tzeferacos et al. 2017, 2018a). The later collision in our platform therefore amplifies and reorganises pre-existing rotational struc-ture, and analogous collision-stage instabilities in related gridded platforms may likewise act on already vortical upstream flows. After the inflows collide, the vortic-ity budget is dominated by stretching and compression, with rstretch ≃ 0.5 and rcomp ≃ 0.45, while baroclinicity drops to rbaro ≃ 0.05 (Figure 9, Figure 10). The velocity spectra show that this shocked layer evolves toward a mixed but predominantly solenoidal cascade. By t/t 0 = 1, the volume-integrated kinetic en-ergy is partitioned approximately 70% in solenoidal mo-tions and 30% in compressive motions (Figure 12), while the scale-by-scale spectral ratio settles to Pc(k)/Ps(k) ∼

0.3 through most of the resolved inertial interval (Fig-ure 13). Over that same range, the total and solenoidal spectra are broadly consistent with k−5/3, whereas the compressive spectrum is steeper, closer to k−2 (Sec-tion 6). The resolved dissipation scale inferred from the strain spectrum is kν, s ≃ 68 mm −1, i.e. ℓν, s ≃ 92 μm, which together with ℓ0 ≃ 4.5 mm implies a resolved scale separation ℓ0/ℓ ν, s ≃ 49 and an effective numeri-cal Reynolds number of order Re ∼ 2 × 10 2 under stan-dard Kolmogorov scaling (Section 6.3, Figure 15, Equa-tion 41). The density statistics retain the imprint of that same shock-turbulence mixture. The mass-density spectrum becomes flatter, from Pρ(k) ∝ k2/3 at t = 151 ns to 

Pρ(k) ∝ k1/6 at t = 302 ns, indicating temporal re-distribution of density structure without evidence for a universal density cascade (Section 7, Figure 16). The spectra of P∇ρ(k), Pk2ρ(k), and P∇·u(k) track one an-other over much of the resolved range, but the ratio 

P∇·u(k)/P∇ρ(k) shows a clear temporal trend: at early time it decreases close to the Kolmogorov-like advec-tive prediction, while by late time it flattens toward the acoustic/source-dominated limit in Equation 54 (Fig-ure 17). Diagnostics sensitive to density gradients there-fore probe the compressive part of the turbulence, tran-sitioning from a more cascade-imprinted regime soon af-ter stagnation toward a more local-divergence-controlled regime as the layer decays; they are not, by themselves, faithful tracers of the underlying incompressible velocity spectrum (Figure 16, Figure 17, Appendix C). Taken together, these results establish the double-mesh platform as a quantitatively characterised labo-ratory realisation of sustained shock-driven turbulence: a 30 ns radiative drive seeds vorticity at the mesh, a collision at 75 ns sets a turbulence outer scale of 

ℓ0 ≃ 4.5 mm, and the layer remains shock-rich and tur-bulently supersonic for at least one outer-scale eddy-turnover time. The velocity field evolves toward a pre-dominantly solenoidal, Kolmogorov-like cascade with an effective resolved Reynolds number of order 2 ×

10 2, while retaining a geometry-imprinted outer-scale anisotropy that is largely confined to the lowest Fourier modes. The scalar density field does not simply follow that incompressible cascade: its gradients remain tied to the compressive velocity divergence, evolving from a more cascade-imprinted regime soon after stagnation to-ward a flatter, source-dominated hierarchy at late time. This combination of persistent outer-scale memory, in-creasingly isotropic inertial-range velocity structure, and compressively controlled density-gradient statistics pro-vides a concrete baseline for future experiments and synthetic-diagnostic studies aimed at intermittency and turbulence in shock-dominated and driven turbulent plasmas, including tests of when isotropy-based proton-radiography inversions are appropriate (Section 3, Sec-tion 4, Section 5, Section 6, Section 7).
